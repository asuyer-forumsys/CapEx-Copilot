# Business Case: Cybersecurity Modernization — Zero Trust Network Access ("Sentrywall ZTNA")

## Executive Summary

Copperline Retail Group operates a store, distribution, and e-commerce footprint that has outgrown the perimeter-based security model on which its enterprise network was originally designed. As of the drafting of this business case in January 2026, the company runs 214 stores across 31 states, four distribution centers in Charlotte NC, Reno NV, Columbus OH, and Allentown PA, and an e-commerce channel that now accounts for roughly 18% of a $1.92B annual revenue base. That operating profile — thousands of endpoints, a hybrid and increasingly remote corporate workforce of approximately 9,600 employees, seasonal third-party labor, and a payment environment subject to PCI DSS — is precisely the profile that the industry's current wave of retail-sector ransomware and credential-theft campaigns is built to exploit.

This business case requests approval to fund a three-year Zero Trust Network Access ("ZTNA") modernization program built on the Sentrywall ZTNA platform, sponsored by Farrukh Rahimi (VP & CISO) with executive sponsorship from Marcus Whitfield (SVP & CIO). The financial model was prepared by Omar Delgado (Director, FP&A). The program carries a three-year total cost of ownership of $10.4M and a projected three-year risk-adjusted benefit of $16.9M, yielding a net present value of $5.1M at the company's standard 8% discount rate and a payback period of 24 months. Because the dominant benefit stream is loss avoidance rather than direct revenue or hard cost takeout, the benefit figure is deliberately risk-adjusted and no internal rate of return is computed; the methodology and rationale for that choice are set out in the Benefits Analysis section below.

The program is structured in two phases. Phase 1 establishes identity-centric access control, multi-factor enforcement, and micro-segmentation for the highest-risk systems — the cardholder data environment, finance and treasury systems, and privileged administrative access. Phase 2 extends coverage across the store and distribution-center fleet, retires the remaining legacy VPN concentrators, and completes device-posture enforcement for the full endpoint population. This case recommends approval of the full two-phase program. As reflected in the Recommendation section, the Tech Investment Steering Committee approved the program at its Q1 2026 meeting on March 12, 2026; Phase 1 is now live and Phase 2 is in progress.

## Problem and Strategic Context

Copperline's network security posture is anchored in a model that assumes a trusted interior and a hostile exterior, mediated by a hardened perimeter. Traffic that originates inside the corporate boundary — or that reaches the interior through a VPN tunnel — is granted broad implicit trust and lateral reachability. That model was defensible when the company's systems were concentrated in a small number of data centers and its workforce sat inside four walls. It no longer reflects how Copperline actually operates.

Three structural shifts have eroded the perimeter model to the point where it now represents the company's most material unmitigated technology risk.

First, the attack surface has expanded and fragmented. The 2025 cloud infrastructure migration moved a substantial share of enterprise workloads out of on-premise data centers and into cloud-hosted environments, which means the "interior" the perimeter was built to protect is now distributed across multiple hosting boundaries the legacy firewalls never contemplated. Every store is a network node with point-of-sale terminals, back-office systems, and increasingly connected operational technology. Every distribution center runs warehouse and materials-handling systems that were historically air-gapped in practice but are now integrated for inventory and orchestration purposes. The flat, implicitly trusted internal network means that a single compromised store endpoint can, in principle, reach systems far outside its blast radius.

Second, the threat environment aimed at retail has intensified in exactly the vectors the perimeter model handles worst. Retail is a preferred target for ransomware operators and for financially motivated intrusion sets because retailers combine payment data, large seasonal fluctuations in staffing and vigilance, and a low tolerance for operational downtime that makes extortion economically attractive. The prevailing intrusion pattern is not a dramatic breach of the perimeter; it is the quiet use of a valid but stolen credential — harvested through phishing, credential stuffing, or a third-party compromise — followed by lateral movement across a trusting internal network toward high-value systems. A perimeter defense has essentially no answer to an adversary who logs in rather than breaks in.

Third, the workforce is permanently hybrid. A material portion of Copperline's corporate, merchandising, and supply-chain staff now works remotely or in a hybrid pattern, and the company's reliance on seasonal and third-party labor spikes the number of transient identities requesting access to internal systems during exactly the peak periods when the business can least afford disruption. The legacy VPN concentrators that mediate remote access grant network-level admission rather than application-specific, continuously evaluated access, which reproduces the same implicit-trust problem for every remote session.

Overlaying all three shifts is a compliance obligation that is becoming less forgiving. As a retailer that stores, processes, and transmits payment card data, Copperline is subject to PCI DSS, whose more recent requirements push firmly toward continuous authentication, strong multi-factor enforcement for access into the cardholder data environment, and demonstrable segmentation of that environment from the rest of the network. The company's cyber-insurance underwriters have likewise moved from treating controls such as enforced MFA, privileged-access management, and segmentation as favorable factors to treating them as preconditions of coverage and pricing. The status quo is therefore not a stable baseline; it is a position that is degrading against both the threat environment and the control expectations of regulators and insurers.

A Zero Trust architecture directly addresses the root cause rather than the symptoms. Its organizing principle — never trust, always verify — replaces implicit interior trust with per-request evaluation of identity, device posture, and context, and replaces broad network reachability with least-privilege access scoped to individual applications. It is the model that the compliance regime, the insurance market, and the threat landscape are all independently converging on.

It is worth being concrete about what the current model costs the company in operational terms, not only in tail risk. Under the perimeter approach, granting a new seasonal hire or a third-party contractor access means admitting them to a broad network zone, which the security team then has little practical ability to constrain or observe at the application level; deprovisioning is correspondingly coarse and slow, so orphaned access accumulates. Every store back-office system that needs to reach a central application does so across a flat internal network that offers no natural chokepoint for inspection. When an incident is suspected, the investigation begins from a position of poor visibility, because the network was never instrumented to answer the question "who accessed what, from what device, and was that access appropriate." Zero Trust does not merely reduce the probability and blast radius of a breach; it converts access into an observable, per-request, revocable event, which improves the company's day-to-day security operations and its ability to satisfy auditors independent of whether any given breach ever occurs. That operational dividend is part of why the case is defensible even before the loss-avoidance argument is scored.

## Financial Modeling

The financial model reflects a three-year total cost of ownership of $10.4M, phased to front-load the platform licensing and Phase 1 implementation effort and to spread the fleet-wide rollout and steady-state operating costs across the remainder of the horizon. The model was prepared by Omar Delgado (FP&A) using the company's standard 8% discount rate and treats FY2026 as Year 1.

The table below summarizes the three-year TCO by cost category. Figures are in USD millions and reflect fully loaded costs, including internal labor allocated to the program.

| Cost category | Year 1 (FY2026) | Year 2 (FY2027) | Year 3 (FY2028) | 3-yr total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sentrywall ZTNA platform licensing and subscription | $1.6 | $1.7 | $1.7 | $5.0 |
| Implementation and integration services | $1.5 | $0.9 | $0.2 | $2.6 |
| Internal program labor (security, network, IAM) | $0.6 | $0.5 | $0.4 | $1.5 |
| Endpoint and device-posture agent rollout | $0.4 | $0.3 | $0.1 | $0.8 |
| Training, change management, and enablement | $0.2 | $0.1 | $0.1 | $0.4 |
| Contingency reserve | $0.1 | $0.0 | $0.0 | $0.1 |
| **Total cost of ownership** | **$4.4** | **$3.5** | **$2.5** | **$10.4** |

Platform licensing is the single largest line and is structured as a multi-year subscription that scales with the number of protected identities and enforcement points rather than with raw seat count, which keeps Year 2 and Year 3 licensing roughly flat even as coverage extends across the full store and distribution-center fleet in Phase 2. Implementation and integration services are concentrated in Year 1, consistent with the front-loaded Phase 1 build. Internal program labor reflects a dedicated core team drawn from the security, network engineering, and identity and access management functions, declining modestly over the horizon as the platform moves from build to steady-state operation. The contingency reserve is intentionally modest at this stage because the Phase 1 scope is well understood; the residual delivery uncertainty is carried in the Risk Assessment rather than inflated into the base budget.

## Benefits Analysis

The economics of a security investment differ in kind from those of a revenue-generating or cost-reduction initiative, and the benefits model is constructed accordingly. The dominant benefit stream is expected loss avoidance — the reduction in the probability-weighted financial impact of security incidents — supplemented by two more directly quantifiable streams: an improved compliance posture that reduces audit and remediation cost, and a favorable movement in cyber-insurance economics. Because the largest component is inherently probabilistic, the entire $16.9M three-year benefit figure is presented on a risk-adjusted basis, and the case does not compute an internal rate of return, which would imply a precision the underlying loss-avoidance estimate does not possess. The net present value of $5.1M at the 8% discount rate and the 24-month payback are therefore the primary financial decision metrics for this case.

The table below summarizes the three benefit streams and the basis for each. Figures are in USD millions and represent risk-adjusted three-year totals.

| Benefit stream | Basis of estimate | 3-yr risk-adjusted benefit |
|---|---|---|
| Incident reduction / expected loss avoidance | Reduction in probability-weighted impact of ransomware, breach, and lateral-movement events, using industry retail-sector incident frequency and severity benchmarks applied to Copperline's revenue and data exposure | $11.8 |
| Compliance posture improvement | Reduced PCI DSS remediation and audit-preparation effort; avoidance of compensating-control and finding-remediation cost; reduced scope through segmentation | $3.2 |
| Cyber-insurance premium and coverage impact | Improved renewal pricing and retained coverage terms attributable to enforced MFA, privileged-access management, and segmentation controls | $1.9 |
| **Total risk-adjusted benefit** | | **$16.9** |

The incident-reduction stream is the analytical heart of the case. It is built by estimating the annualized expected loss from the incident categories that Zero Trust most directly mitigates — credential-driven intrusion, lateral movement, and the ransomware and data-exfiltration outcomes those enable — and then estimating the reduction in that expected loss attributable to the control set. Expected loss is the product of event likelihood and event severity, and Zero Trust reduces both: least-privilege access and micro-segmentation shrink the reachable blast radius when a credential is compromised, while continuous verification and device-posture checks reduce the likelihood that a compromised credential succeeds in the first place. The estimate deliberately uses conservative, benchmark-derived frequencies rather than worst-case single-event figures, because the purpose of the model is to justify a sustained control investment, not to price a catastrophe.

The compliance stream is more concrete. Segmentation of the cardholder data environment reduces PCI DSS scope, which reduces the population of systems subject to the standard's controls and the corresponding audit and evidence-gathering burden every assessment cycle. Scope reduction compounds: fewer in-scope systems means fewer controls to evidence, fewer compensating controls to maintain, and fewer findings to remediate each cycle, so the saving recurs annually rather than resolving to a one-time cleanup. The insurance stream is the most concrete of the three: it reflects the difference between the renewal terms Copperline can expect with enforced Zero Trust controls in place versus the deteriorating pricing and coverage-restriction trajectory the company faces without them. Underwriters increasingly treat enforced MFA, privileged-access management, and segmentation not as discounted line items but as gating questions on the application; the realistic counterfactual for a retailer of Copperline's profile that fails to implement them is not merely a higher premium but a narrower policy with more exclusions and a higher retention. All three streams are presented net of the operating costs already captured in the TCO, and none of them double-counts the loss-avoidance stream: compliance and insurance savings are cost and coverage effects, distinct from the probability-weighted incident losses the segmentation and least-privilege controls avoid.

## Risk Assessment

This case is evaluated against Copperline's five standard risk-assessment categories.

**Implementation overrun.** The principal delivery risk is that Phase 2 — the fleet-wide extension across 214 stores and four distribution centers, and the retirement of legacy VPN concentrators — proves more complex and labor-intensive than the Phase 1 build for high-risk systems suggests. Rollout across a heterogeneous store estate with varying local network conditions and legacy endpoints is a classic source of schedule and cost slippage. This risk is rated moderate. It is mitigated by the phased structure itself, which converts a single large cutover into a sequenced rollout that can be paced to capacity and learning, and by concentrating the contingency and the most experienced internal labor on the Phase 2 fleet work.

**Benefit-realization shortfall.** Because the largest benefit stream is loss avoidance, realization is inherently difficult to prove — an incident that does not happen leaves no ledger entry. The risk is that the program is fully funded and delivered but its value is never credibly demonstrated, weakening support for sustained operating investment. This risk is rated moderate. It is mitigated by tracking leading indicators the program can actually move — reduction in standing privileged access, segmentation coverage of the cardholder data environment, MFA enforcement rates, and mean time to contain — and by reporting insurance and compliance outcomes, which are directly observable, as the concrete anchor for the harder-to-observe loss-avoidance claim.

**Data-quality degradation.** Zero Trust decisions are only as good as the identity and device data that feed them; stale identity records, orphaned accounts, and incomplete device inventories will produce both false denials that frustrate users and false grants that undermine the control. This risk is rated moderate-to-high, because it is a precondition for the platform to function as intended rather than a peripheral concern. It is mitigated by treating identity hygiene and device-inventory remediation as explicit, resourced Phase 1 workstreams rather than assumptions, and by establishing recurring reconciliation between the identity source of truth and the enforcement layer.

**Adoption failure.** Zero Trust changes the daily access experience for the entire workforce, and poorly managed enforcement — excessive prompts, opaque denials, or friction during peak operating periods — can drive workarounds, shadow access paths, and organizational resistance that hollow out the control. This risk is rated moderate. It is mitigated by phased enforcement that begins with the highest-risk systems and privileged users rather than the whole population at once, by tuning policies against real access patterns before hard enforcement, and by an enablement and change-management workstream funded explicitly in the TCO.

**Vendor viability.** The program concentrates significant strategic dependence on a single platform vendor, Sentrywall, which creates exposure to the vendor's financial health, product roadmap, and support quality over the multi-year horizon. This risk is rated low-to-moderate. It is mitigated by the subscription structure, which limits sunk upfront capital, by contractual commitments on support and data portability, and by an architecture that keeps identity as the durable control plane so that enforcement tooling could be substituted without re-architecting the underlying access model.

## Scenario Modeling

The base case reflects the figures presented above: a $10.4M three-year TCO, $16.9M in risk-adjusted benefit, a $5.1M NPV at 8%, and a 24-month payback. It assumes Phase 1 delivers on schedule, Phase 2 rolls out across the fleet without material disruption, identity and device-data remediation proceeds as a funded workstream, and the insurance and compliance benefits materialize on the expected trajectory.

The optimistic scenario assumes a benign-to-favorable delivery and benefit environment: Phase 2 rollout runs ahead of the paced plan and slightly under budget as the store-level playbook stabilizes; the insurance market rewards the completed control set more generously at renewal than the base case assumes; and the avoided-loss estimate proves conservative because the segmentation and least-privilege controls demonstrably contain one or more incidents that would otherwise have propagated. In this scenario the three-year risk-adjusted benefit rises toward the low-$20M range, TCO lands modestly below $10.4M, and the payback period compresses toward the low-20-month range. No IRR is computed even in the optimistic case, for the same reason it is not computed in the base case.

The pessimistic scenario assumes delivery friction and muted benefit realization: Phase 2 overruns on both schedule and cost as store-estate heterogeneity proves harder to standardize than expected; identity and device-data remediation consumes more effort than budgeted, delaying reliable enforcement; and adoption friction generates workarounds that dilute the effective control coverage. In this scenario TCO drifts above $11M, the risk-adjusted benefit falls toward the low-teens of millions as loss-avoidance credit is discounted for incomplete coverage, and the payback period extends past 30 months. Critically, even in this scenario the program remains NPV-positive, and the compliance and insurance benefit streams — the two most concrete and least contingent — continue to accrue, which is the central reason the case treats the downside as tolerable rather than disqualifying.

## Recommendation and Governance

The recommendation is to approve the full two-phase Sentrywall ZTNA modernization program at the $10.4M three-year TCO. The case rests on three propositions that hold across the scenario range. First, the perimeter-based model is a deteriorating position, not a stable baseline, and the cost of inaction rises as the threat, compliance, and insurance environments continue to move against it. Second, the program is NPV-positive at the 8% discount rate even under pessimistic delivery and realization assumptions, and its two most concrete benefit streams — compliance and insurance — accrue independently of the harder-to-prove loss-avoidance claim. Third, the phased structure meaningfully bounds the delivery risk by converting a single large transformation into a sequenced, capacity-paced rollout with an early, high-value Phase 1.

Governance for the program follows Copperline's standard framework. The Tech Investment Steering Committee, co-chaired by the CFO and CIO, is the approving body. Because the $10.4M three-year TCO is spread across the horizon and no single fiscal-year capital ask exceeds the $5M threshold that triggers Board Finance & Audit Committee ratification, the program did not require separate board ratification and was approved at the committee level. The Steering Committee approved the program at its Q1 2026 meeting on March 12, 2026, with a disposition of approved. As of the current status, Phase 1 is live — identity-centric access control, MFA enforcement, and micro-segmentation for the cardholder data environment, finance and treasury systems, and privileged access are in production — and Phase 2, the fleet-wide extension and legacy VPN retirement, is in progress. The program will be reported through the standard portfolio benefits-tracking cadence, with leading control indicators and the concrete compliance and insurance outcomes serving as the reporting anchors given the loss-avoidance nature of the primary benefit. Ongoing oversight will transition the program into post-implementation monitoring once Phase 2 completes fleet-wide rollout.
