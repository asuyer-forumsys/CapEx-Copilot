# Internal Audit Memorandum: Technology Investment Portfolio Risk Review

## Scope and Objectives of Review

This memorandum is issued by Grace Muthoni, VP Internal Audit and Chief Audit Executive, on May 18, 2026, to the members of the Tech Investment Steering Committee. It presents the findings of Internal Audit's review of the governance and risk posture of Copperline Retail Group's active technology investment portfolio. The review was conducted as a portfolio-level risk assessment rather than a controls audit of any single system, and it is advisory in nature: its purpose is to surface risks the committee should be tracking, not to render an opinion on the design or operating effectiveness of individual application controls.

The scope covered the initiatives active or recently completed as of the review date: the ERP Replacement program, the Cloud Infrastructure Migration, the Omnichannel POS / Unified Commerce program, the Cybersecurity Modernization — Zero Trust program, and the Customer Data Platform and AI Personalization program. The two initiatives that had not yet reached the committee for decision at the time of this review — the WMS + Robotics Pilot and the AI Customer Service Chatbot — were outside the scope of this memo. The review objectives were threefold: to assess whether the risks identified in each business case remain adequately tracked as the programs progress; to identify emerging risks not fully reflected in the approved cases; and to observe portfolio-level patterns in how risk is being estimated and managed across the cases. The review drew on the business cases and their financial models, the completed post-implementation reviews for Cloud Migration and POS, the Business Case Review Policy owned by the Enterprise PMO, and interviews with program sponsors and the FP&A function.

Internal Audit assessed risk against the five standard risk-assessment categories used throughout the portfolio: implementation overrun, benefit-realization shortfall, data-quality degradation, adoption failure, and vendor viability. The findings below are organized first by initiative and then by cross-cutting portfolio-level observation.

## Findings by Initiative

The table below summarizes the primary risk finding for each in-scope initiative, the category it falls under, and Internal Audit's risk rating. Narrative detail on the most significant findings follows.

| Initiative | Primary risk finding | Category | IA rating |
|---|---|---|---|
| ERP Replacement | Integration and data-migration complexity likely understated relative to legacy environment; early warning of scope exposure | Implementation overrun / data-quality degradation | High |
| Cloud Infrastructure Migration | Opex-savings realization still below target as of the January PIR; benefit trajectory not yet complete | Benefit-realization shortfall | Moderate |
| Omnichannel POS / Unified Commerce | Training-completion gap driving revenue-lift shortfall; adoption assumptions not met | Adoption failure | Moderate-High |
| Cybersecurity Modernization (ZTNA) | Loss-avoidance benefit inherently hard to verify; dependence on identity/device data quality | Benefit-realization shortfall / data-quality degradation | Moderate |
| Customer Data Platform (CDP) | Benefit case depends on cross-channel adoption in an organization with a recent adoption shortfall | Adoption failure | Moderate |

**ERP Replacement — data-migration and integration risk.** Internal Audit's most significant finding concerns the ERP Replacement program. The original October 2024 business case scoped a defined set of legacy integrations and carried a data-migration plan predicated on the documented state of the incumbent Summit environment. Our review found that the reliability of that documented state is itself a material risk. The incumbent environment is poorly documented, and poorly documented legacy systems characteristically conceal integration and data dependencies that surface only during detailed implementation mapping. We flag, as a forward-looking risk rather than a confirmed overrun, that the program's integration surface and data-migration effort may prove materially larger than the approved case assumes, and we recommend the committee treat the current budget and timeline as carrying meaningful downside exposure pending completion of integration discovery. This is the highest-rated finding in the review. (Note for the reader of this memo in sequence: this finding predates, and anticipates, the integration scope-creep discovery that was subsequently confirmed to the committee later in 2026.)

**Cloud Infrastructure Migration — benefit realization below target.** The Cloud Infrastructure Migration completed its twelve-month post-implementation review in January 2026. That review reported opex-savings realization at 92% of the $7.1M annual target. Internal Audit's finding is not that the program is failing — a 92% realization at the twelve-month mark is a reasonable trajectory — but that the benefit case is not yet fully realized and should not be treated as closed. We recommend the committee continue to track realization forward through the benefits cadence rather than retiring the program's tracking on the strength of a favorable but incomplete PIR. The risk is one of premature closure: declaring victory before the last several points of realization are demonstrably banked.

**Omnichannel POS / Unified Commerce — adoption and training gap.** The POS program completed its six-month PIR in February 2026, which reported the omnichannel revenue lift at approximately 58% of pace — a material shortfall against the case. The PIR root-caused the shortfall substantially to training-completion gaps: the frontline behavioral change on which the revenue lift depended was not adopted at the rate the case assumed, because the enablement did not reach completion across the store estate. Internal Audit rates this a moderate-to-high adoption-failure finding, and treats it as the portfolio's clearest realized instance of the adoption risk that several other cases carry. Our concern is less the shortfall itself than what it reveals: a case that projected benefits contingent on frontline adoption did not build or track the enablement rigorously enough to realize them.

**Cybersecurity Modernization and CDP.** For the two programs approved at the Q1 2026 committee, Internal Audit's findings are anticipatory rather than realized. For Cybersecurity, we note that the program's dominant benefit is loss avoidance, which is inherently difficult to verify after the fact, and that the control's effectiveness depends on the quality of identity and device data feeding it; we regard the program's stated intent to track leading control indicators as the appropriate mitigation and recommend the committee hold it to that. We further observe that the decision not to compute an IRR for the cybersecurity case is, from an audit perspective, the correct treatment rather than a gap: a loss-avoidance benefit that is genuinely probabilistic should not be dressed in a rate-of-return figure that implies false precision, and we would have flagged the opposite choice as a concern. The residual risk we do flag is that the loss-avoidance benefit, being unobservable when the control succeeds, creates a standing temptation to under-fund the program's steady-state operation once the initial build is complete; the committee should guard against treating an uneventful year as evidence the control is over-resourced. For the CDP, we note that its benefit case, like the POS case before it, depends on cross-channel adoption within an organization that has just demonstrated an adoption shortfall, and we recommend the committee verify that the adoption and enablement milestones it directed at approval are concrete, resourced, and tracked rather than nominal. The proximity of the CDP approval to the POS PIR is, in our view, an opportunity: the committee has a fresh, well-documented example of exactly how an adoption-dependent benefit can fall short, and the CDP delivery plan should be measured against that example specifically.

## Portfolio-Level Observations

Beyond the initiative-specific findings, Internal Audit observed patterns that cut across the portfolio and that the committee should weigh at the portfolio level rather than case by case.

The most important pattern is a systematic optimism in adoption assumptions. Across multiple cases, benefit realization is contingent on behavioral change — frontline staff adopting new workflows, the organization operating new capabilities as designed — and the estimates for the pace and completeness of that adoption tend toward the optimistic end. The POS shortfall is the realized proof of this pattern, and the CDP and, prospectively, other adoption-dependent cases carry the same latent exposure. This is not a criticism of any single sponsor; it is a structural tendency in how benefit cases are constructed, and it argues for a standard expectation that adoption-dependent benefits be paired with funded, tracked enablement plans and with more conservative ramp assumptions.

A second pattern is the reliance on legacy-environment documentation whose completeness is not independently verified. The ERP finding is the sharpest example, but the general point applies wherever a case's cost and risk estimates rest on the assumed state of an incumbent system. Where that assumed state is not independently validated, the case carries an unpriced discovery risk.

A third, more favorable observation is that the portfolio's governance framework is functioning as designed. Business cases are reviewed against a consistent policy and template, dispositions are recorded against a defined vocabulary, financial models use a consistent 8% discount rate and a common preparer in FP&A, and post-implementation reviews are actually being conducted and are surfacing real shortfalls rather than rubber-stamping success. The optimism and documentation risks above are risks within a sound process, not symptoms of an absent one.

## Recommendations

Internal Audit makes the following recommendations to the Steering Committee.

First, on the ERP program, complete integration and data-migration discovery on an accelerated basis and treat the current budget and timeline as carrying material downside until that discovery is complete; do not let the approved envelope harden into an assumption. Where discovery confirms additional scope, route the revision through the change-request process rather than absorbing it silently.

Second, adopt a standard expectation that any business case whose benefits depend on adoption pair those benefits with a funded, milestone-tracked enablement plan and with ramp assumptions that reflect the organization's demonstrated adoption pace, including the POS experience. The committee should be able to see, at approval, how each adoption-dependent benefit will actually be realized.

Third, continue tracking benefit realization on completed programs through the benefits cadence rather than retiring tracking at the PIR, so that programs like Cloud Migration are held to full realization rather than closed on a favorable-but-incomplete reading.

Fourth, for benefit cases that rest on the assumed state of legacy systems, require independent validation of that assumed state, or an explicit discovery-risk reserve, before treating the case's cost estimate as firm.

## Management Response

Management, represented by Elena Popescu (Director, Enterprise PMO) on behalf of the PMO function, acknowledges the findings and concurs with the thrust of the recommendations. The PMO notes that the Business Case Review Policy already provides for the change-request mechanism recommended for the ERP program and commits to applying it rigorously as ERP discovery progresses. The PMO further concurs that adoption-dependent benefit cases warrant funded and tracked enablement plans, and notes that the adoption and enablement milestones directed for the CDP at its approval reflect this direction; it will work to make that expectation standard across future cases. The PMO concurs with continued benefits tracking beyond the PIR and will maintain the recurring benefits cadence accordingly. A fuller management response, including target dates, will be provided through the committee's standard action-item tracking.
