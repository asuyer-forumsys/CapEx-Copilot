# Business Case: Omnichannel Point-of-Sale and Unified Commerce Platform

## Executive Summary

This business case requests capital and operating authorization to replace Copperline Retail Group's fragmented in-store point-of-sale (POS) estate with a single cloud-native unified commerce platform spanning all 214 stores across 31 states, the e-commerce storefront, and the four distribution centers in Charlotte NC, Reno NV, Columbus OH, and Allentown PA. The initiative is sponsored by Sasha Kim, VP Digital Commerce, with financial modeling prepared by Omar Delgado, Director of FP&A. It is submitted to the Tech Investment Steering Committee for review in the first-quarter cycle, following the co-chairs' guidance from the December 2024 committee that any omnichannel proposal must show a self-funding profile within three years and a defensible path to closing the buy-online-pickup-in-store (BOPIS) experience gap against regional peers.

The recommended program carries a three-year total cost of ownership (TCO) of $9.6M, against a projected three-year benefit of $13.4M. Using the corporate 8% discount rate mandated for all capital evaluations, the initiative returns a net present value of $2.6M, an internal rate of return of 24%, and a simple payback of 26 months. Because the single-year capital ask does not exceed the $5M threshold that triggers mandatory Board Finance & Audit Committee ratification in any one fiscal year, the decision rests with the Steering Committee, though the co-chairs may elect to brief the Board for visibility given the customer-facing nature of the change.

The strategic thesis is straightforward. Copperline sells home and garden merchandise where the purchase journey is increasingly cross-channel: a customer researches a patio set or a seasonal live-goods assortment online, checks availability at a nearby store, and completes the transaction in whichever channel is most convenient at the moment of decision. Today that journey is served by three disconnected systems that do not share inventory truth, cannot originate a sale in one channel and fulfill it in another, and impose material checkout latency during the spring and holiday peaks that define our seasonal business. The unified commerce platform collapses those systems into one transactional core, giving associates and customers a single, real-time view of inventory and a consistent checkout regardless of channel. The financial return is driven primarily by incremental omnichannel revenue, secondarily by checkout labor efficiency and inventory accuracy improvements, and is sensitive above all to store-associate adoption of the new selling motions — a dependency this case flags explicitly and addresses in the risk and scenario sections.

The recommendation is to approve the program for a phased rollout, with contracting and pilot deployment beginning immediately upon approval, a controlled pilot across a representative store cohort, and general availability across the fleet targeted for late summer. The remainder of this document sets out the problem and strategic context, the financial model, the quantified benefits, a structured risk assessment across the five standard categories, scenario modeling, and the governance recommendation.

## Problem and Strategic Context

Copperline's current in-store transaction environment is the accreted result of two decades of point solutions, a prior ownership change, and the 2019–2021 acquisition of two regional garden-center chains whose stores were never fully re-platformed. The practical consequence is a legacy POS estate that is fragmented along three fault lines, each of which imposes a distinct and measurable cost on the business.

The first fault line is the POS software itself. Roughly two-thirds of the fleet runs an aging on-premise POS release that reached its vendor's extended-support cliff, while the remainder — largely the acquired garden centers — runs a different package entirely. Neither talks natively to the e-commerce platform. Register software is patched store-by-store, price and promotion changes propagate on an overnight batch cycle rather than in real time, and a pricing correction issued from the Charlotte headquarters can take up to a full business day to reach every lane. During promotional events this lag produces register-level price discrepancies that associates resolve manually, eroding both margin discipline and customer trust at the exact moments — spring garden season, holiday décor — when transaction volume is highest.

The second fault line is the absence of real-time inventory visibility across channels. Store inventory, distribution-center inventory, and the quantity-available figure shown on the website are reconciled on separate cadences and stored in separate systems. The website displays an availability estimate that can be hours stale; a store associate checking stock for a customer is looking at a different number than the customer saw online that morning; and the distribution centers cannot see committed store demand in time to influence replenishment. The result is a double failure mode: we disappoint customers by showing online availability for items a store cannot actually locate, and we lose sales by hiding, out of conservatism, inventory we in fact hold. Cycle-count discipline varies widely by store, and measured inventory record accuracy sits in the low-to-mid 80s percent range fleet-wide against a specialty-retail benchmark closer to the mid-90s.

The third fault line is transactional: the systems cannot originate a sale in one channel and fulfill it in another without manual intervention. Buy-online-pickup-in-store, which for a home-and-garden retailer is not a convenience feature but a core expectation for bulky and seasonal goods, is today a stitched-together process. An online order routes to a store as an email-and-print pick ticket; associates re-key details; there is no reliable digital handshake confirming the customer's item is staged and paid; and returns of online orders to a store frequently cannot be processed against the original transaction. "Endless aisle" — the ability for an associate to sell an out-of-stock item from another store or a distribution center and have it shipped to the customer — does not exist in any systematic form. Every one of these gaps represents revenue we are structurally unable to capture.

Layered on top of these structural gaps is a performance problem that is most acute precisely when it is most expensive. Median in-store checkout time currently runs at 94 seconds per transaction. During peak periods, slow registers, manual price overrides, and disconnected loyalty lookups lengthen queues, and abandoned-basket incidents at the front end rise. Checkout latency is not merely a labor-cost line; in a seasonal business, queue abandonment during the spring rush is directly lost revenue that never appears in any system.

The disconnection also imposes a hidden cost on the distribution network. The four distribution centers in Charlotte, Reno, Columbus, and Allentown replenish stores against demand signals that are batch-delayed and channel-blind. They cannot see committed BOPIS demand in time to influence a store's replenishment, cannot rebalance inventory to where cross-channel demand is actually surfacing, and cannot support endless-aisle fulfillment because no system routes a store-originated sale to the nearest stocking location. The result is a supply chain that is optimized for a single-channel store model in a business that is no longer single-channel — carrying safety stock where demand is not, and stocking out where it is. A unified inventory truth is the precondition for the distribution centers to fulfill against real, channel-aware demand.

The strategic context is equally clear. E-commerce is approximately 18% of Copperline's $1.92B FY2025 net revenue and is the fastest-growing channel, but its growth is capped by the store network's inability to participate in digital fulfillment. Regional and national competitors have largely completed unified-commerce transitions and now market same-day BOPIS and cross-channel returns as standard. For a PE-recapitalized specialty retailer whose equity story depends on disciplined margin expansion alongside modest top-line growth, closing this gap is not optional. The unified commerce platform is the foundational enabler for the customer-data and personalization investments the Digital Commerce organization intends to bring forward in subsequent cycles; without a single transactional core producing clean, channel-tagged customer and inventory data, those downstream investments cannot be underwritten credibly.

## Financial Modeling

The financial model was prepared by Omar Delgado, Director of FP&A, using Copperline's standard three-year TCO convention and the mandated 8% discount rate. Costs are presented on a fully-loaded basis, inclusive of software subscription, implementation and systems integration, in-store hardware refresh, training and change management, and allocated internal program labor. Benefits are treated separately in the following section and flow into the return metrics summarized in the Executive Summary.

The three-year total cost of ownership is $9.6M, distributed as follows.

| Cost Category | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | 3-Year Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Unified commerce software subscription (POS + order management) | $1.2M | $1.3M | $1.3M | $3.8M |
| Implementation and systems integration | $2.1M | $0.4M | $0.2M | $2.7M |
| In-store hardware refresh (terminals, scanners, receipt/label printers) | $1.4M | $0.2M | $0.1M | $1.7M |
| Training and change management | $0.5M | $0.2M | $0.1M | $0.8M |
| Allocated internal program labor | $0.3M | $0.15M | $0.15M | $0.6M |
| **Total** | **$5.5M** | **$2.25M** | **$1.85M** | **$9.6M** |

The cost profile is deliberately front-loaded. Year 1 absorbs the bulk of implementation, integration, and hardware spend because the platform must be stood up, integrated to the ERP and e-commerce systems, and physically deployed to registers across the fleet before benefit realization can begin. The recurring software subscription steps up modestly as stores are cut over and additional order-management modules are activated, then stabilizes. Systems-integration spend declines sharply after the initial build, retaining a maintenance tail for interface tuning. Hardware is overwhelmingly a Year 1 event, with small subsequent amounts covering break-fix replacement and late-cohort stores.

Two modeling conventions warrant note. First, the internal-labor line reflects only incremental allocated effort — program management, integration testing, and store-readiness coordination — and does not attempt to charge back general IT run-rate cost. Second, the model does not capitalize the software subscription; it is treated as operating expense consistent with the cloud-native delivery model, while implementation and hardware are treated as capital in line with Copperline's capitalization policy. The blended presentation here is for TCO comparability and does not alter the accounting treatment applied in the general ledger.

Against this cost base, the three-year projected benefit of $13.4M yields a net present value of $2.6M at the 8% discount rate, an internal rate of return of 24%, and a simple payback of 26 months. The payback period sits deliberately beyond the two-year mark because the front-loaded cost profile and the ramp-dependent nature of the omnichannel revenue benefit push the crossover into the third year of operation. This timing is a material feature of the case and is stress-tested in the Scenario Modeling section.

For comparison against the broader technology portfolio, this profile sits in the middle of the range the Steering Committee is evaluating: a payback longer than the cloud-infrastructure and cybersecurity programs but shorter than the ERP replacement, and a return that is respectable rather than spectacular. That is the appropriate expectation for a foundational commerce platform whose value is unlocked over time and through downstream investments, rather than captured in a single step. The case does not rest on aggressive assumptions to clear the hurdle rate; it clears an 8% discount rate with margin to spare even before the strategic option value of the clean customer and inventory data it produces is considered — value that the Digital Commerce organization intends to monetize through subsequent personalization investments and does not attempt to credit here.

## Benefits Analysis

The benefit case rests on four quantified levers. The largest by far is incremental omnichannel revenue; checkout efficiency and inventory accuracy contribute meaningful but smaller amounts; and associate training is presented as a benefit-enabling cost line rather than a source of savings, included here for transparency because the entire revenue lever is contingent on it.

| Benefit Metric | Baseline | Target (Year 3 exit) | 3-Year Value Contribution |
|---|---|---|---|
| Median checkout time per transaction | 94 sec | 75 sec (−20%) | $2.1M (front-end labor efficiency + reduced peak abandonment) |
| Omnichannel / BOPIS incremental revenue (margin contribution) | Minimal structured BOPIS | Fleet-wide BOPIS + endless aisle | $9.4M |
| Inventory record accuracy | ~83% | ~94% | $1.9M (reduced shrink, fewer false-stockouts, lower safety stock) |
| Associate "endless aisle" training completion | 0% | 90% of stores | (enabling investment, costed in TCO) |
| **Total quantified benefit** | | | **$13.4M** |

The omnichannel revenue lever is the heart of the case. Copperline captures very little structured cross-channel revenue today because the transactional plumbing does not exist. The unified commerce platform enables three concrete revenue mechanisms: reliable BOPIS that converts online browsers who prefer to collect bulky items themselves; endless-aisle selling that lets an associate close a sale for an item the local store does not stock; and cross-channel returns that keep customers and their subsequent spend inside the Copperline network rather than driving a refund-and-defect. The model sizes the incremental margin contribution conservatively against the e-commerce channel's current scale and the seasonal skew of the assortment, and assumes a graded ramp rather than a step-change, reaching full run-rate only in the third year. This ramp assumption is the single most important sensitivity in the case, because the revenue lever depends entirely on store associates actually using the new selling motions.

The checkout-time lever converts the 94-second baseline toward a 75-second target, a 20% reduction driven by modern register hardware, real-time price and promotion accuracy that eliminates manual overrides, and integrated loyalty and payment lookups. The value is booked partly as front-end labor efficiency and partly as recovered peak-season revenue from shorter queues. The model is deliberately restrained here; it does not assume headcount reduction, only redeployment of freed associate time toward selling and service.

The inventory-accuracy lever moves record accuracy from roughly 83% toward roughly 94%. Real-time, channel-unified inventory reduces shrink, cuts the false-stockout losses where we hide sellable inventory, and permits a modest reduction in safety stock without service degradation. The value here is grounded in current carrying cost and shrink figures and is intentionally the most conservative of the three revenue-bearing levers.

The final row, associate training completion, is not a savings source; it is the adoption precondition for the revenue lever, surfaced here because its cost is embedded in the TCO training line and because its completion rate is the leading indicator the post-implementation review will scrutinize.

## Risk Assessment

The following assessment addresses the five standard risk categories applied to every Copperline business case.

**Implementation overrun.** The program touches every store, both distribution and headquarters systems, and physical hardware at the register. The principal overrun risk lies in integration complexity between the new platform, the in-flight ERP replacement, and the e-commerce storefront, and in the logistics of a 214-store hardware deployment. The risk is assessed as moderate. Mitigations include a fixed-scope statement of work with the platform vendor, a phased cohort rollout that contains blast radius, a contingency reserve embedded in the Year 1 integration and hardware lines, and explicit interface-freeze coordination with the ERP program to avoid moving-target integration.

**Benefit-realization shortfall.** Because roughly 70% of the benefit is ramp-dependent omnichannel revenue, the dominant financial risk is that the revenue lever ramps more slowly than modeled. The risk is assessed as moderate-to-high and is the case's defining exposure. Mitigations include the conservative graded-ramp assumption already baked into the base case, quarterly benefit tracking against the ramp curve, and a remediation trigger tied to associate-adoption metrics rather than to revenue alone, so that a shortfall is detected and acted upon before it compounds.

**Data-quality degradation.** Migrating pricing, product, loyalty, and inventory data from three legacy systems into one core creates a one-time risk of dirty or duplicated records, and an ongoing risk if store-level inventory hygiene does not improve alongside the technology. The risk is assessed as moderate. Mitigations include pre-migration data cleansing, reconciliation gates at each cohort cutover, and a tightened cycle-count cadence introduced as part of the store-readiness playbook.

**Adoption failure.** The platform can be flawless and still fail to deliver if associates do not adopt endless-aisle and BOPIS selling motions. The risk is assessed as high and is tightly coupled to the benefit-realization risk above. Mitigations center on the training and change-management investment, on making the new motions the path of least resistance in the register UX, on store-manager incentives tied to omnichannel participation, and on treating training-completion percentage as a governed program metric rather than a soft target.

**Vendor viability.** The initiative concentrates the transactional core on a single unified-commerce vendor. The risk is assessed as low-to-moderate. Mitigations include selecting an established platform with a broad specialty-retail reference base, contractual data-portability and source-code-escrow provisions, and an architecture that isolates integrations behind a stable interface layer so that a future platform change would not require re-integrating every surrounding system.

## Scenario Modeling

To test the durability of the return, the model was run under three scenarios that flex the two most sensitive drivers — the pace of the omnichannel revenue ramp and implementation cost — while holding the 8% discount rate and the general cost structure constant.

| Scenario | Key assumptions | 3-Year Benefit | 3-Year Cost | NPV (8%) | Payback |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Base | Graded revenue ramp to full run-rate in Year 3; cost on plan | $13.4M | $9.6M | $2.6M | 26 mo |
| Optimistic | Faster adoption, revenue ramp pulled forward ~2 quarters; cost on plan | $15.8M | $9.6M | $4.3M | 21 mo |
| Pessimistic | Adoption lags, revenue ramp reaches only ~65% of plan; ~5% cost overrun | $9.7M | $10.1M | $0.4M | 34 mo |

The base case is the recommended planning basis and reflects Omar Delgado's central estimate. The optimistic case assumes that associate adoption exceeds plan — training completes ahead of schedule and endless-aisle selling becomes habitual quickly — pulling the revenue ramp forward by roughly two quarters and improving both NPV and payback materially. The pessimistic case is deliberately harsh on the case's known weak point: it assumes adoption lags, the revenue ramp reaches only about two-thirds of the modeled level, and implementation runs modestly over budget. Even under that combination the program remains marginally NPV-positive, though payback slips past the three-year window. The asymmetry of the scenarios — a strong upside and a floor that stays barely above breakeven — reflects a program whose downside is bounded by the hard-benefit levers (checkout efficiency and inventory accuracy) while its upside is governed by adoption. This is the correct risk shape for a foundational platform investment, and it reinforces the recommendation to govern adoption aggressively.

## Recommendation and Governance

The recommendation is to approve the Omnichannel POS and Unified Commerce Platform for a phased fleet rollout, at a three-year TCO of $9.6M, on the base-case financials of $13.4M benefit, $2.6M NPV, 24% IRR, and 26-month payback. Because the single-year capital component remains below the $5M threshold, the decision falls within the Steering Committee's authority and does not require Board Finance & Audit Committee ratification; the co-chairs may nonetheless elect to brief the Board given the customer-facing scope.

The sponsor requests approval on the following conditions, which map directly to the risk assessment. First, that the program adopt training-completion percentage as a governed, board-visible adoption metric, with a Year-3-exit target of 90% of stores completing endless-aisle training. Second, that benefit realization be tracked quarterly against the ramp curve, with an explicit remediation trigger if adoption metrics fall behind, so that a benefit shortfall is caught early. Third, that interface-freeze coordination with the in-flight ERP replacement be formalized to contain integration risk. Fourth, that a post-implementation review be scheduled approximately six months after general availability to validate the return and, critically, to inspect the adoption assumption on which the majority of the benefit depends.

Sponsorship rests with Sasha Kim, VP Digital Commerce. The financial model is owned by Omar Delgado, Director of FP&A. On the strength of this case, the Steering Committee is asked to record a disposition of approved and to authorize immediate contracting and pilot deployment, with general availability across the 214-store fleet targeted for late summer. Upon approval this document becomes the baseline of record against which the six-month post-implementation review will measure outcomes, including the deliberate scrutiny of whether the omnichannel revenue ramp materializes at the pace the adoption plan assumes.
