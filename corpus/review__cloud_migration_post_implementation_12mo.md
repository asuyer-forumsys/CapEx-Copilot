# Post-Implementation Review: Cloud Infrastructure Migration (12-Month)

## Purpose and Scope of Review

This post-implementation review (PIR) evaluates the Cloud Infrastructure Migration program twelve months after its January 2025 go-live, measuring actual outcomes against the projections in the original business case approved by the Tech Investment Steering Committee in November 2024. The review was prepared by Omar Delgado (Director, FP&A), owner of the program's financial model and benefits baseline, in collaboration with Marcus Whitfield (SVP & CIO), the program's accountable sponsor. It is presented to the Steering Committee, co-chaired by Renata Alvarez (EVP & CFO) and Marcus Whitfield, as the formal validation checkpoint required as a condition of the original approval.

The scope of this review covers the three benefit and cost dimensions that the original business case identified as its core, measurable commitments: unplanned-downtime reduction, data-center operating-cost savings, and one-time implementation cost. Each is assessed against its original baseline and target, with a verdict, followed by a root-cause narrative explaining variances in both directions. The review deliberately reports the full picture — outcomes that beat plan, outcomes still ramping, and a genuine cost overrun — rather than a selective summary, consistent with Copperline's business-case governance standards.

This is a twelve-month checkpoint, not a program closeout. Several benefits (notably operating-cost savings tied to legacy decommissioning) are still ramping toward their steady-state run-rate, and the review's forward-looking sections address how those trajectories will be tracked to full realization.

## Outcomes vs. Original Projections

The table below compares each core metric against its original-case baseline and target, with the actual measured at the January 2026 review point and a verdict. Downtime figures are quarterly; opex figures are annual run-rate; implementation cost is the one-time program total.

| Metric | Baseline | Original Target | Actual (Jan 2026) | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Unplanned downtime (hrs/quarter) | 14.0 | 6.3 (−55%) | 5.46 (−61%) | Overperformed |
| Data-center opex savings (run-rate) | $9.4M/yr | $7.1M/yr run-rate (−$2.3M/yr) | 92% of target savings realized (≈$2.1M/yr; run-rate ≈$7.3M/yr) | On track, still ramping |
| Implementation cost (one-time) | — | $6.8M | $7.4M (+8.8%) | Overrun |

Read together, the three lines tell an honest and, on balance, favorable story. Availability materially beat plan: unplanned downtime fell to 5.46 hours per quarter against a 6.3-hour target, a 61% reduction versus the 55% committed. Operating-cost savings are tracking well but have not yet reached full run-rate — 92% of the targeted $2.3M/yr saving was realized as of the review, with the remainder gated on completing legacy decommissioning. And the one-time implementation cost overran, landing at $7.4M against a $6.8M target, an 8.8% overrun. None of these outcomes undermines the original investment thesis; the availability overperformance and the on-track savings trajectory more than offset the contained implementation overrun, and the program's realized economics remain consistent with the approved NPV, IRR, and payback.

It is worth being precise about the interaction among the three lines, because they are not independent. The implementation overrun and the availability overperformance share a common root — additional network-resilience investment — and should be read as a single decision with two consequences rather than as a coincidental pairing of one bad outcome and one good one. The savings shortfall, by contrast, is unrelated to either: it is a pure timing effect driven by the pace of legacy shutdown, and it is closing on its own as decommissioning proceeds. Separating a genuine timing lag from a genuine benefit erosion is central to reading this PIR correctly, and the distinction is developed in the root-cause narrative that follows.

## Root-Cause Narrative

### Why downtime overperformed

The 61% reduction in unplanned downtime — better than the 55% committed — is attributable primarily to the resilience of the target architecture performing better in production than the business case conservatively assumed. The migration landed critical workloads on a multi-availability-zone design with automated failover, replacing the manual, infrequently tested failover of the legacy secondary site. Two effects compounded. First, automated failover eliminated most of the human-response latency that had previously turned recoverable events into extended outages. Second, the elimination of aging on-premise hardware removed an entire class of hardware-failure incidents that had contributed materially to the 14-hour baseline. The original case valued the availability benefit conservatively; the production result exceeded that conservative estimate, which is the most favorable kind of variance.

### Why implementation cost overran

The $0.6M overrun (from a $6.8M target to $7.4M actual, +8.8%) has a specific and defensible root cause: unplanned network redundancy work that was not fully scoped in the original estimate. As the migration progressed, the program identified that achieving the resilient, multi-AZ availability posture at the reliability level the business required demanded additional network redundancy and connectivity engineering beyond the original design — redundant inter-region and on-ramp connectivity, and hardening of the network path between remaining on-premise systems and the cloud environment. This work was not discretionary scope creep; it was necessary to deliver the DR and availability outcomes the case promised. Notably, this same investment is a direct contributor to the downtime overperformance described above. In other words, the overrun and the availability beat are two sides of the same decision: Copperline spent modestly more than planned on network resilience and got materially better availability than planned in return.

The retrospective lesson for future business cases is a scoping one rather than a delivery one. The original estimate under-provisioned for the network engineering required to reach the target availability posture, treating connectivity and redundancy as a smaller line item than they proved to be. The program made the correct call in the moment — funding the resilience work rather than compromising the availability commitment — and the 8.8% overrun is the visible price of that call. Framed against a benefit stream anchored on availability and disaster recovery, spending $0.6M more to comfortably beat the downtime target was sound economics, and FP&A regards it as a favorable trade rather than a delivery failure. Future infrastructure cases should scope network resilience more generously up front.

### Why operating-cost savings are still ramping

Operating-cost savings reached 92% of the targeted $2.3M/yr run-rate reduction as of the review — a strong result, but short of the full target because legacy data-center decommissioning has proceeded more slowly than the original plan assumed. The opex savings benefit is mechanically dependent on shutting down legacy facilities and hardware: the run-rate reduction is only fully realized once the corresponding on-premise systems are decommissioned and their facilities, maintenance, and support costs are eliminated. A residual set of legacy workloads — a mix of systems with longer migration lead times and a small number awaiting downstream dependencies from adjacent modernization programs — remained in service at the on-premise footprint at the twelve-month mark, carrying a tail of run-rate cost that has not yet been eliminated. This is a timing variance, not a benefit-erosion variance: the savings are expected to continue improving beyond the 92% realization as the remaining decommissioning completes, and internal tracking already shows the trajectory advancing past this review's measurement point.

The distinction matters for how the Committee should read the number. A benefit-erosion variance would mean the migration is structurally incapable of delivering the promised savings — that the $2.3M/yr target was wrong. That is not what the evidence shows. The migrated workloads are running on the cloud at their expected cost; the shortfall is entirely the residual on-premise cost that persists until the corresponding legacy systems are switched off. Every system decommissioned removes another slice of that residual, moving realization toward and eventually past 100% of target as the tail clears. The 92% figure is therefore best understood as a snapshot of an in-progress decommissioning schedule at the twelve-month mark, not as a ceiling on what the migration can achieve. The one genuine lesson is that the original plan was optimistic about decommissioning pace, in part because some legacy shutdowns are entangled with dependencies owned by adjacent modernization programs still in flight.

## Ongoing Monitoring and Next Steps

The program transitions from active delivery into continued benefits monitoring, with FP&A retaining ownership of the benefits baseline. Three items carry forward:

**Complete legacy decommissioning.** The primary open item is finishing the shutdown of the residual on-premise workloads that are gating the final portion of the opex savings. Enterprise IT will maintain a decommissioning schedule with dates for each remaining system, and FP&A will track the resulting run-rate savings against the $2.3M/yr target until full realization. This is expected to move the savings realization above the current 92% in subsequent tracking periods.

**Sustain the availability gains.** The downtime overperformance should be monitored to confirm it is durable rather than a favorable early-period artifact. Quarterly availability reporting will continue against the original 6.3-hour target, with attention to whether the multi-AZ posture holds up through a full seasonal peak cycle.

**Hand off to portfolio benefits tracking.** Ongoing realization will be folded into Copperline's portfolio-level benefits tracker, so that the cloud migration's continued opex savings and availability performance are reported alongside the rest of the modernization portfolio rather than through a standalone program mechanism. This ensures the remaining ramp is visible to the Steering Committee without a dedicated program-review overhead.

The handoff also carries a lesson worth propagating to the rest of the portfolio. The single most useful discipline this program applied was tying the opex-savings benefit explicitly to a decommissioning schedule with named systems and dates, rather than treating the saving as an automatic consequence of go-live. That discipline is exactly what allows this review to state with confidence that the 92% realization is a timing position rather than a shortfall, and to point to the specific systems whose shutdown will close the gap. Adjacent modernization initiatives that carry legacy-retirement benefits — most notably the ERP replacement now in flight — would do well to adopt the same practice, because a savings target with no decommissioning schedule behind it is a target with no mechanism to hold it accountable. FP&A intends to carry this convention into how it structures benefit baselines across the portfolio.

## Recommendation

The review recommends that the Steering Committee accept the Cloud Infrastructure Migration as a successful implementation and place it in **post-implementation monitoring**, with no further capital ask required at this time. The program has delivered on its core commitments: availability materially exceeded plan, operating-cost savings are on a healthy trajectory at 92% of target with a clear path to full realization, and the one-time cost overran by a contained and well-explained 8.8% that is directly linked to the network-resilience investment responsible for the availability overperformance. The realized economics remain consistent with the original approved case.

No corrective action is recommended beyond completing the legacy-decommissioning schedule already in motion, which is expected to close the remaining opex-savings gap through the normal course of the program's wind-down. FP&A will continue to report realization through the portfolio benefits tracker, and the Committee will receive updated figures in the ordinary tracking cycle rather than through a further dedicated review. Marcus Whitfield remains accountable sponsor for the residual monitoring; Omar Delgado retains ownership of the benefits baseline and the tracking hand-off.
