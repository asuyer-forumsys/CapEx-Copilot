# Business Case: Customer Data Platform and AI Personalization ("Lumenpoint CDP")

## Executive Summary

This business case requests authorization to procure and implement Lumenpoint CDP, a customer data platform with an integrated AI personalization capability, to unify Copperline Retail Group's fragmented customer data and convert it into measurable revenue through personalized merchandising, targeted lifecycle marketing, and improved customer retention. The initiative is sponsored by Sasha Kim, VP Digital Commerce, with financial modeling prepared by Omar Delgado, Director of FP&A. It is submitted to the Tech Investment Steering Committee for the first-quarter 2026 review cycle.

The program carries a three-year total cost of ownership of $7.1M against a projected three-year benefit of $11.8M. At the mandated 8% discount rate, it returns a net present value of $3.4M, an internal rate of return of 29%, and a simple payback of 20 months. The single-year capital component sits below the $5M Board ratification threshold, placing the decision within the Steering Committee's authority.

Lumenpoint CDP is the logical successor investment to the Omnichannel POS and Unified Commerce Platform approved in February 2025 and now live across the fleet. That program built the transactional core that finally produces clean, channel-tagged purchase data; this program puts that data to work. The strategic thesis is that Copperline holds rich but siloed signals about its customers — in-store purchase history, e-commerce browsing and cart behavior, and loyalty-program activity — and today cannot assemble them into a single identity, cannot act on them in real time, and therefore competes on a generic, one-size-fits-all customer experience against peers who personalize. Lumenpoint resolves the customer identity across channels, activates it through AI-driven recommendations and lifecycle campaigns, and is expected to lift conversion, deepen email and loyalty engagement, and increase customer lifetime value.

The dominant risk is data quality: a personalization engine trained on fragmented, duplicated, or stale customer records will produce poor recommendations that erode rather than build trust. This case treats data-quality degradation as its defining exposure and builds the mitigation into the program rather than bolting it on. The recommendation is to approve the initiative, with a target go-live in the first quarter of 2027.

## Problem and Strategic Context

Copperline knows a great deal about its customers and can act on almost none of it coherently. Customer signal today lives in three principal silos that do not share a common identity. The point-of-sale and unified-commerce environment now captures in-store and cross-channel purchase history at the transaction level. The e-commerce platform holds browsing behavior, search queries, cart composition, and abandonment events. The loyalty program maintains membership, points balances, tier status, and redemption history. A single customer who shops in a store on Saturday, browses the website on Sunday, and redeems loyalty points the following week appears as three unrelated actors to Copperline's systems. There is no persistent, resolved customer identity that stitches those interactions together.

The consequences are concrete. Marketing is broadly segmented and largely calendar-driven rather than behavior-driven; email campaigns go out to wide audiences with generic offers because the data to target them narrowly is not assembled in an actionable form. The website presents the same merchandising to a first-time visitor and a ten-year loyalty member. We cannot reliably suppress promotions to customers who would have purchased anyway, nor time an outreach to a customer whose purchase cycle — seasonal live goods, annual patio refresh — is predictable from history if only the history were joined. Personalization, where it exists, is rule-based and shallow: a handful of hand-maintained segments rather than a model that learns from behavior.

The competitive context makes this gap costly. Copperline's e-commerce channel is approximately 18% of the company's $1.92B FY2025 net revenue and is the growth engine of the business, but its conversion rate trails specialty-retail benchmarks in part because the on-site experience is undifferentiated. Competitor benchmarking commissioned by the Digital Commerce organization found that leading home-and-garden and broader specialty retailers now treat cross-channel personalization as table stakes: resolved customer identity, real-time recommendation surfaces, triggered lifecycle journeys, and loyalty offers tuned to individual purchase patterns. Copperline competes against these capabilities with generic merchandising and batch email, and the conversion and retention gap is the measurable result.

The timing is deliberate. Until the unified commerce platform went live, Copperline's in-store purchase data was too fragmented and batch-delayed to serve as a reliable personalization input. With that foundation now in place, the customer data it produces is clean, channel-tagged, and timely enough to underwrite a CDP investment credibly. Lumenpoint is therefore not a speculative add-on but the intended second step of a sequenced digital strategy: build the transactional core first, then activate its data.

The seasonal and category structure of Copperline's business makes personalization unusually well-suited to the assortment. Home-and-garden purchasing is highly cyclical and highly predictable at the individual level: a customer who buys live goods each spring, a patio refresh every few years, or holiday décor each winter follows a pattern that a model can learn and anticipate. Generic, calendar-based marketing treats every customer as if they sit at the same point in that cycle; a resolved identity with purchase history lets Copperline reach each customer at the moment their own cycle predicts a purchase. The upside from getting this right is larger for a seasonal specialty retailer than for a general merchant, because the timing of the outreach — not merely its content — is where much of the value sits. This is the specific mechanism by which the CDP is expected to convert Copperline's rich but siloed history into revenue that the current undifferentiated approach leaves on the table.

## Financial Modeling

The financial model was prepared by Omar Delgado, Director of FP&A, on Copperline's standard three-year TCO basis at the 8% discount rate. The total cost of ownership is $7.1M, distributed as follows.

| Cost Category | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | 3-Year Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lumenpoint CDP software subscription | $1.0M | $1.1M | $1.1M | $3.2M |
| Implementation and data integration | $1.4M | $0.3M | $0.2M | $1.9M |
| AI personalization / ML tooling and activation | $0.5M | $0.4M | $0.4M | $1.3M |
| Data governance, privacy and consent management | $0.2M | $0.1M | $0.1M | $0.4M |
| Allocated internal labor | $0.1M | $0.1M | $0.1M | $0.3M |
| **Total** | **$3.2M** | **$2.0M** | **$1.9M** | **$7.1M** |

The cost profile front-loads implementation and data-integration effort in Year 1, when the platform must be stood up and connected to the POS/unified-commerce, e-commerce, and loyalty source systems, and when the initial identity-resolution and data-cleansing work is performed. The software subscription steps up modestly as data volumes and activated use cases grow, then stabilizes. The personalization and ML-tooling line is more evenly spread because model development, testing, and iterative activation of recommendation and lifecycle use cases continue through all three years. Notably, the data-governance line is funded from Year 1 forward rather than deferred, reflecting the case's judgment that privacy, consent management, and data-quality controls are prerequisites to activation, not afterthoughts.

Against this cost base, the three-year benefit of $11.8M yields an NPV of $3.4M, an IRR of 29%, and a payback of 20 months. The sub-two-year payback is stronger than the POS program's because the CDP builds on an existing data foundation rather than deploying physical infrastructure, and because its benefit levers — conversion and engagement — begin contributing earlier once the first use cases go live.

## Benefits Analysis

The benefit case rests on three quantified levers, all downstream of resolved customer identity and activated personalization.

| Benefit Metric | Baseline | Target (Year 3 exit) | 3-Year Value Contribution |
|---|---|---|---|
| E-commerce conversion rate | Undifferentiated baseline | +personalized merchandising and recommendations | $6.5M |
| Email / loyalty engagement (open, click, repeat-purchase rate) | Broad batch campaigns | Behavior-triggered lifecycle journeys | $3.1M |
| Customer lifetime value (retention and repeat frequency) | Generic experience | Personalized retention and reactivation | $2.2M |
| **Total quantified benefit** | | | **$11.8M** |

The conversion lever is the largest contributor. Personalized on-site merchandising and AI-driven recommendations — surfacing the right seasonal assortment, relevant complementary items, and individually-tuned offers — are expected to lift the e-commerce conversion rate off its undifferentiated baseline. Because e-commerce is roughly 18% of a $1.92B revenue base, even a modest percentage-point improvement in conversion produces meaningful incremental margin, and the model sizes this lever conservatively against current traffic and basket economics.

The engagement lever converts broad, calendar-driven batch email and loyalty communications into behavior-triggered lifecycle journeys: replenishment reminders timed to a customer's purchase cycle, post-purchase cross-sell, abandonment recovery, and loyalty offers tuned to individual patterns. The value is booked as incremental repeat-purchase revenue from higher engagement rates.

The lifetime-value lever captures retention and reactivation: personalized experiences that increase repeat frequency and re-engage lapsing customers extend the revenue-producing life of the customer base. This lever is the most conservative of the three because retention effects compound over a longer horizon than the three-year model window fully credits.

All three levers share a single precondition — accurate, resolved customer data — which is why the following risk section treats data quality as the case's central exposure.

## Risk Assessment

The following assessment addresses the five standard risk categories.

**Implementation overrun.** The program integrates three source systems into a new platform and stands up identity resolution and ML tooling. Overrun risk concentrates in the data-integration and identity-resolution work of Year 1. The risk is assessed as moderate. Mitigations include a fixed-scope implementation statement of work, sequencing source-system integration so that the highest-quality data (the now-clean POS/unified-commerce feed) is onboarded first, and a contingency reserve in the Year 1 integration line.

**Benefit-realization shortfall.** The benefit depends on activated use cases actually lifting conversion and engagement. The risk is assessed as moderate. Mitigations include a use-case roadmap that front-loads the highest-confidence, fastest-activating levers (on-site recommendations, abandonment recovery), controlled A/B measurement of each activated use case so that lift is proven rather than assumed, and quarterly benefit tracking against the ramp.

**Data-quality degradation.** This is the defining risk of the case and is assessed as high. A personalization engine is only as good as the customer data beneath it; fragmented, duplicated, or stale records produce mistargeted recommendations and mistimed outreach that erode customer trust and can actively depress conversion. The exposure is both one-time (dirty data migrated at onboarding) and ongoing (identity resolution drifting as new records arrive). Mitigations are built into the program rather than bolted on: a dedicated data-cleansing and deduplication workstream ahead of activation; identity-resolution match-rate and record-completeness metrics governed as program KPIs; ongoing data-quality monitoring with alerting on drift; and a deliberate decision to fund data governance from Year 1. The case explicitly refuses to activate personalization use cases against source data that has not passed defined quality gates.

**Adoption failure.** Unlike the POS program, adoption here is less about frontline behavior change and more about marketing and merchandising teams operationalizing the new capability. The risk is assessed as moderate. Mitigations include embedding the CDP into existing campaign and merchandising workflows, enablement for the marketing organization, and executive sponsorship from Digital Commerce to drive use-case adoption.

**Vendor viability.** The program concentrates customer data and personalization on the Lumenpoint platform. The risk is assessed as low-to-moderate. Mitigations include selecting an established CDP vendor with a credible enterprise reference base, contractual data-portability and export guarantees so that the resolved customer data remains Copperline's asset, and an architecture that keeps the underlying data store accessible independent of the vendor's activation layer.

## Scenario Modeling

The model was flexed under three scenarios, varying the pace and magnitude of the conversion and engagement lift and the data-quality readiness that gates activation, holding the 8% discount rate constant.

| Scenario | Key assumptions | 3-Year Benefit | 3-Year Cost | NPV (8%) | Payback |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Base | Use-case roadmap on plan; data quality passes gates on schedule | $11.8M | $7.1M | $3.4M | 20 mo |
| Optimistic | Faster conversion lift, additional use cases activated; cost on plan | $14.2M | $7.1M | $5.0M | 16 mo |
| Pessimistic | Data-quality remediation delays activation ~2 quarters; ~5% cost overrun | $8.9M | $7.5M | $1.2M | 27 mo |

The base case is the recommended planning basis. The optimistic case assumes conversion lift exceeds plan and the team activates additional high-value use cases within the window. The pessimistic case models the case's specific weak point: data-quality problems delay activation by roughly two quarters and drive a modest cost overrun in remediation. Even so, the program remains solidly NPV-positive, reflecting a benefit base that is diversified across three levers and does not hinge on a single ramp assumption to the degree the POS case did. The scenario spread confirms that the principal threat to value is not demand for the capability but readiness of the data — which is precisely where the program concentrates its mitigation and governance.

## Recommendation and Governance

The recommendation is to approve Lumenpoint CDP and AI Personalization at a three-year TCO of $7.1M, on base-case financials of $11.8M benefit, $3.4M NPV, 29% IRR, and 20-month payback. The single-year capital component is below the $5M threshold, so the decision rests with the Steering Committee without Board Finance & Audit Committee ratification.

The sponsor requests approval on the following conditions, mapped to the risk assessment. First, that data-quality gates — identity-resolution match rate and record completeness — be defined and governed as program KPIs, with a standing commitment not to activate personalization use cases against source data that has not cleared those gates. Second, that each activated use case be measured with controlled A/B testing so that conversion and engagement lift is proven rather than asserted, with quarterly benefit tracking against the ramp. Third, that data governance, privacy, and consent management be funded and staffed from Year 1, consistent with the cost model. Fourth, that the program sequence its source-system integration to onboard the clean POS/unified-commerce data first.

Sponsorship rests with Sasha Kim, VP Digital Commerce; the financial model is owned by Omar Delgado, Director of FP&A. The Tech Investment Steering Committee reviewed this case at its first-quarter 2026 meeting on March 12, 2026, and recorded a disposition of approved, authorizing contracting and implementation to begin, with go-live targeted for the first quarter of 2027. Upon that go-live the program will enter the standard quarterly benefits-tracking cadence, and a post-implementation review will be scheduled to validate the return once the personalization use cases have had time to ramp.
