# Post-Implementation Review: Omnichannel POS and Unified Commerce Platform (6-Month)

## Purpose and Scope of Review

This post-implementation review (PIR) evaluates the Omnichannel POS and Unified Commerce Platform approximately six months after its general availability across Copperline Retail Group's 214-store fleet. The program was approved by the Tech Investment Steering Committee in February 2025, sponsored by Sasha Kim, VP Digital Commerce, with the financial model prepared by Omar Delgado, Director of FP&A. It reached general availability in August 2025. This review is authored by Omar Delgado with Sasha Kim and is submitted into the standard PIR cadence for approved capital initiatives.

The purpose of the review is to compare realized outcomes against the projections recorded in the approved business case, to root-cause the variances in both directions, to set out a remediation plan where realized performance trails plan, and to make a governance recommendation on continued monitoring. The review is intentionally candid: it reports one metric that has materially overperformed, one that has fallen short of its ramp, and one that has run modestly over budget. The scope covers the three headline metrics carried in the original benefits and financial models — median checkout time, omnichannel revenue lift, and implementation cost — measured as of the six-month mark. It does not attempt a full three-year benefit reconciliation, which is premature at this stage; the omnichannel revenue benefit in particular was always modeled as a graded ramp reaching full run-rate only in Year 3, so the relevant question at six months is whether realization is tracking the ramp curve, not whether the full three-year figure has been achieved.

Data sources for the review are the transaction and register telemetry from the unified commerce platform, the finance system of record for capitalized and expensed program cost, the store-operations training-completion records, and the omnichannel revenue tags now produced natively by the platform. All figures are reconciled to the finance close as of the review date.

A note on method is warranted given that this is the first PIR to draw on data the platform itself generates. Prior to the program, omnichannel revenue could not be measured cleanly because the transactional systems did not tag revenue by channel of origination and fulfillment; the figures in this review are therefore the first channel-attributed revenue numbers Copperline has been able to produce natively, and they are correspondingly more reliable than the pre-program estimates the original case had to work from. Checkout-time measurement is drawn from register telemetry across the full transaction population rather than sampled observation, so the median reported here reflects actual fleet behavior rather than a study window. Cost figures are actuals to the finance close, not accruals. Where this review expresses a verdict, it is grounded in these measured sources rather than in operational impression.

## Outcomes vs. Original Projections

The table below compares realized outcomes at the six-month mark against the baselines and targets recorded in the approved business case. The omnichannel revenue figure is expressed as realized-to-date against the pace the approved ramp curve expected by this point, not against the full three-year target.

| Metric | Baseline | Original Target | Actual (Feb 2026 PIR) | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Median checkout time per transaction | 94 sec | 75 sec (−20%) | 62 sec (−34%) | Overperformed |
| Omnichannel revenue lift (realized to date) | Minimal structured BOPIS | On-pace toward $13.4M over 3 years | $3.9M (~58% of expected ramp pace) | Shortfall |
| Implementation cost | — | $9.6M | $9.9M (+3%) | Minor overrun |

Three findings stand out. Checkout time has beaten its target decisively: against a 94-second baseline and a 75-second target, the fleet is running at a median of 62 seconds, a 34% reduction versus the 20% that was promised. Omnichannel revenue is behind pace: $3.9M realized to date represents roughly 58% of what the approved ramp curve expected to have been captured by this point, a clear shortfall relative to plan even accounting for the graded-ramp design. Implementation cost came in at $9.9M against a $9.6M plan, a 3% overrun that is minor in absolute terms and within the range the risk assessment anticipated. Each verdict is root-caused below.

## Root-Cause Narrative

### Checkout time — why it overperformed

The 34%-versus-20% outperformance on checkout time is attributable to a combination of hardware and user-experience factors that, in aggregate, exceeded the assumptions in the original case. The modern register hardware deployed as part of the program — faster terminals, integrated scanners, and quicker receipt and label printing — reduced per-transaction mechanical time more than the model credited. More significantly, the elimination of manual price and promotion overrides had a larger effect than anticipated: because the unified commerce platform propagates pricing in real time rather than on the legacy overnight batch cycle, the register-level price discrepancies that used to force associates into time-consuming manual corrections during promotional events largely disappeared. Integrated loyalty and payment lookups, which previously required separate steps and system switches, now resolve in a single flow. The original case modeled these improvements conservatively and did not assume they would compound; in practice they did, and the median transaction is materially faster than target across both the core fleet and the former garden-center stores. This is a genuine, durable operational win and the clearest success of the program to date.

### Omnichannel revenue lift — why it fell short

The revenue shortfall is the central finding of this review, and its root cause is specific and addressable: store-associate adoption of the new omnichannel selling motions has lagged because training completion lagged. The original business case identified adoption as the program's defining risk and set a plan of 90% of stores completing "endless aisle" training. At the six-month mark, only 64% of stores have completed that training. The gap between the 90% plan and the 64% actual is the proximate cause of the revenue miss.

The mechanism is direct. The omnichannel revenue benefit — BOPIS conversion, endless-aisle selling of items the local store does not stock, and cross-channel returns that retain customers — depends on associates actively using capabilities the platform now provides. Where training has been completed, the selling motions are being used and the revenue is materializing broadly in line with expectation. Where training has not been completed, associates default to the old behaviors: they do not offer endless-aisle fulfillment, they process BOPIS hesitantly, and they route cross-channel returns as refunds-and-defections. The platform is technically performing as designed; the revenue is not being captured because a third of the fleet is not yet equipped to capture it. This is not a technology failure and not a demand failure — customer appetite for BOPIS and endless aisle is evident in the stores that are executing — it is an adoption-and-training execution gap, exactly the risk the case flagged and exactly the sensitivity that drove the pessimistic scenario.

It is worth noting for governance purposes that the base-case financials never assumed full revenue capture at six months; the ramp was always graded toward a Year-3 run-rate. The concern is not that the full $13.4M has not appeared — it was never expected to by now — but that realization is running at roughly 58% of the pace the ramp curve required, which if left uncorrected would push the trajectory toward the pessimistic scenario rather than the base case.

The variance across stores is instructive and reinforces the diagnosis. When the fleet is split between stores that have completed endless-aisle training and those that have not, the completed cohort is capturing omnichannel revenue at a per-store pace broadly consistent with the base-case ramp, while the uncompleted cohort is capturing a small fraction of that pace. The shortfall is therefore not distributed evenly as a general softness in demand; it is concentrated in the untrained third of the fleet. That concentration is the strongest available evidence that the root cause is training completion rather than any weakness in the underlying commercial thesis, and it is what gives the remediation plan its confidence: the intervention has a demonstrated conversion path from training to revenue in the stores that have already made the transition.

### Implementation cost — why it ran modestly over

Implementation cost finished at $9.9M against a $9.6M plan, a 3% overrun. The variance is concentrated in integration and store-readiness effort: coordinating interface work against the in-flight ERP replacement required more integration-testing cycles than budgeted, and late-cohort store cutovers absorbed incremental hardware break-fix and change-management support. The overrun sits within the contingency logic of the original model and within the range the implementation-overrun risk contemplated. It is not a driver of concern in its own right and does not materially alter the program's return; it is reported here for completeness and honesty.

## Remediation Plan

The remediation plan targets the single root cause that matters for the return — the training-completion gap — and is designed to bring the omnichannel revenue trajectory back toward the base-case ramp before the shortfall compounds.

First, an accelerated store-associate training push will close the gap between the current 64% completion and the 90% plan. The push prioritizes the stores with the highest omnichannel demand signal and the largest realized gap between potential and captured revenue, so that training effort flows first to where it converts fastest. Completion will be sequenced by cohort with a defined schedule rather than left to store discretion, and store managers will be accountable for their store's completion status.

Second, training-completion percentage is elevated from a soft target to a governed program metric, reported into the quarterly benefits tracker alongside the revenue figure, so that the leading indicator (training) and the lagging indicator (revenue) are reviewed together and a future divergence is caught early.

Third, the omnichannel selling motions will be reinforced beyond the initial training event: refresher enablement, in-register prompts that make endless-aisle and BOPIS the path of least resistance, and store-manager incentives tied to omnichannel participation, so that completed training translates into sustained behavior rather than a one-time certification.

Fourth, the revenue-lift trajectory will be re-forecast once the accelerated training push has had a full quarter to take effect, to establish whether realization is returning to the base-case ramp or whether the three-year benefit projection needs to be revised.

The remediation plan does not call for any change to the technology, the vendor, or the platform configuration, because none of those is the cause of the shortfall. It is deliberately narrow: fix the adoption gap, govern the leading indicator, and re-measure.

## Recommendation

The recommendation of this review is to continue the program in post-implementation monitoring, execute the remediation plan on the training gap, and revisit the revenue-lift trajectory at the next quarterly benefits tracker.

The program's fundamentals are sound. The technology is performing — checkout time has beaten target decisively, and the platform is producing the clean, channel-tagged data that the subsequently-approved Lumenpoint CDP program depends upon. The cost overrun is minor and within tolerance. The one material shortfall, omnichannel revenue realization, has a clear, specific, and addressable root cause in training completion, and there is direct evidence from the stores that have completed training that the benefit materializes where the adoption precondition is met. This is the best possible shape for a shortfall: not a broken assumption about demand or technology, but an execution gap on a lever the organization controls.

Accordingly, no change to the program's disposition is recommended at this time; it remains in post-implementation monitoring. The next quarterly benefits tracker should report training completion against the 90% target and omnichannel revenue against the base-case ramp, and should determine whether the accelerated training push has restored the trajectory. If, after a full quarter of remediation, realization has not returned toward the base-case ramp, the three-year benefit projection should be formally re-forecast at that point. The checkout-time outperformance should be recognized as a durable win and factored into the run-rate benefit base.
