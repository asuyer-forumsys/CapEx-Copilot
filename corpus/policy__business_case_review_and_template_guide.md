# Business Case Review and Template Guide

## Purpose and Applicability

This guide defines how Copperline Retail Group, Inc. evaluates, approves, and monitors technology and capital investments. It is owned and maintained by the Enterprise Project Management Office (PMO) under Elena Popescu, Director, Enterprise PMO, and it applies to every proposed initiative that requires capital expenditure, multi-year operating commitments, or material change to Copperline's technology, supply-chain, or customer-facing platforms. In practice this covers any initiative sponsored by a functional leader and brought before the Tech Investment Steering Committee, whether the ask is a full platform replacement or a scoped pilot.

The policy exists to ensure that investment decisions across our 214 stores, four distribution centers (Charlotte NC, Reno NV, Columbus OH, and Allentown PA), and e-commerce channel are made on a consistent, comparable, and defensible basis. Because Copperline is a PE-recapitalized business with disciplined capital allocation expectations, every sponsor is expected to justify an ask against the same financial standards, the same risk vocabulary, and the same disposition language. A business case that does not conform to this template will be returned by the PMO before it reaches the Steering Committee agenda.

Applicability is deliberately broad. Sponsors should not attempt to structure an initiative to fall below a threshold or to avoid review; the PMO evaluates substance over form. When a program is delivered in phases, the full multi-year program — not the individual phase — determines applicability and the approval threshold. The Cybersecurity Modernization program, for example, is governed as a single case even though it is delivered in phases.

## Business Case Required Sections

Every business case submitted at Copperline must follow the standard seven-section template. The sections are ordered to move a reviewer from context to recommendation without backtracking, and each has mandatory content.

**Executive Summary.** A one-page synthesis stating the ask, the sponsor, the total three-year cost of ownership, the projected three-year benefit, the headline return metrics (NPV, IRR where computed, and payback), and the requested disposition. This section must be legible on its own to a Board member who reads nothing else.

**Problem and Strategic Context.** The business problem being solved and its linkage to Copperline's strategy — store growth, e-commerce penetration (currently roughly 18% of revenue), margin protection at our ~9.1% EBITDA level, or risk reduction. This section must articulate the cost of inaction, not merely the appeal of action.

**Financial Modeling.** The full quantitative case: three-year TCO built up by cost category, the benefit model with stated assumptions, and the computed NPV, IRR, and payback. All figures must reconcile to the model prepared by FP&A (Omar Delgado is the designated model preparer for portfolio cases), and every material assumption must be documented and sourced.

**Benefits Analysis.** A disaggregation of the projected benefit into named, measurable components — opex savings, revenue lift, avoided cost, or risk reduction — each with a baseline, a target, and the mechanism by which it will be realized and later tracked. Benefits that cannot be measured post-implementation should not be counted in the headline number.

**Risk Assessment.** An assessment against the five mandatory risk categories (defined below), with likelihood, impact, and mitigation for each. Reviewers treat an incomplete risk section as a disqualifying gap.

**Scenario Modeling.** At minimum a base, downside, and upside case, showing how NPV and payback move under changes to the key value drivers (typically adoption rate, benefit ramp, and implementation cost). This section is where the sensitivity of the recommendation is made explicit.

**Recommendation and Governance.** The sponsor's recommended disposition, any proposed conditions, the governance and reporting cadence that will apply after approval, and the named accountable owner for benefit realization.

Sponsors should treat these sections as cumulative rather than independent. The Benefits Analysis must reconcile to the benefit line used in the Financial Modeling; the Scenario Modeling must flex the same value drivers named in the Benefits Analysis and the Risk Assessment; and the Recommendation must follow from, not contradict, the downside case. A case in which the scenario section shows a fragile NPV but the recommendation asserts confidence will be returned. Where a section does not apply — for example, IRR for a risk-adjusted security case — the sponsor states why it does not apply rather than omitting it silently.

## Approval Thresholds and Governance

The Tech Investment Steering Committee is the standing body that reviews business cases. It is co-chaired by Renata Alvarez (EVP & CFO) and Marcus Whitfield (SVP & CIO) and meets quarterly, in approximately mid-March, mid-June, mid-September, and mid-December. Sponsors bring cases to the Committee for review and disposition; the Committee may approve, attach conditions, defer, or reject.

Any single-year capital ask exceeding $5M additionally requires ratification by the Board Finance & Audit Committee, chaired by Independent Director Harold Whitcombe. Steering Committee approval of such a case is a recommendation to the Board, not a final authorization; the ask is not funded until the Board committee ratifies. This two-step path applies to the largest initiatives in the portfolio, including the ERP Replacement and the Cybersecurity Modernization program.

Every disposition is recorded using Copperline's standard vocabulary, and sponsors and minute-takers must use these terms precisely:

- **approved** — the case is authorized as presented.
- **approved-with-conditions** — authorized subject to stated conditions that must be satisfied and tracked (for example, a staged funding release or a required control).
- **deferred** — not decided at this sitting; returned to a future cycle, typically with guidance on what must change. The WMS + Robotics Pilot was deferred to FY2027 Q2 on this basis.
- **rejected** — declined; the initiative does not proceed in its current form. The AI Customer Service Chatbot was rejected.
- **superseded-by-addendum** — the original case remains the historical record but is replaced for governance purposes by an approved change request. This is the status the original ERP case takes once its addendum is ratified.
- **in post-implementation monitoring** — the initiative is live and under active benefit tracking and post-implementation review obligations.

The annual budget cycle frames these decisions. Budget planning runs August through October, and the Board approves the next fiscal year's budget in December; Copperline's fiscal year equals the calendar year. Cases that depend on next-year funding should be timed so that a disposition is reached before the December Board approval. A case deferred at the September sitting can, in practice, still be reworked and brought to the December sitting in time for inclusion in the following year's budget, but a case that first appears in December has little room for the iteration the Committee expects. Sponsors are therefore encouraged to bring material asks to the June or September cycles, leaving one quarter of runway for conditions to be addressed before the budget closes.

The co-chairs may attach a quorum or recusal requirement to any decision involving a sponsor's own function. Where the CFO or CIO is also the sponsor of a case — as is common given the size of the technology portfolio — the other co-chair leads that item, and the Board Finance & Audit Committee's ratification role provides the independent check for asks above the $5M threshold.

## Financial Standards

To keep cases comparable, Copperline mandates uniform financial conventions, which are described in full in the companion financial methodology reference and summarized here.

All NPV calculations use an **8% discount rate**. No sponsor may substitute a project-specific hurdle rate; the single mandated rate is what makes cross-portfolio NPV rankings meaningful.

The standard evaluation horizon is **three years** for both TCO and projected benefit. TCO must be built from the standard cost categories — licensing, implementation, integration, data migration, training, infrastructure, and contingency — so that no material cost is omitted or buried. Payback is reported in months.

IRR is reported where a conventional benefit stream makes it meaningful. For investments whose value is expressed primarily as risk reduction rather than a cash benefit stream — the Cybersecurity Modernization case is the standing example — IRR may be reported as not applicable, and the case is judged on NPV and risk-adjusted benefit instead.

Every Risk Assessment section must address exactly these five categories, and only these, so that risk is comparable across cases:

1. **implementation overrun** — cost or schedule exceeding plan during delivery.
2. **benefit-realization shortfall** — realized benefits falling below the modeled target.
3. **data-quality degradation** — data integrity or migration risk undermining the outcome.
4. **adoption failure** — users or stores not adopting the capability at the assumed rate.
5. **vendor viability** — the risk that a selected vendor cannot sustain the relationship.

## Post-Implementation Review Requirements

Approval is not the end of governance. Where an initiative goes live, it enters post-implementation review (PIR). Copperline requires a PIR at **6 months** and again at **12 months** after go-live, where applicable to the initiative's profile. The PIR compares realized cost and benefit against the approved case, identifies variances and their root causes, and records lessons for future cases. The Cloud Infrastructure Migration completed its 12-month PIR in January 2026; the Omnichannel POS / Unified Commerce initiative completed its 6-month PIR in February 2026.

Beyond the scheduled PIRs, every live initiative with tracked benefits carries an **ongoing quarterly benefits-tracking obligation**. FP&A maintains a portfolio benefits tracker on a quarterly cadence, reporting each tracked metric against baseline and target with a Red/Amber/Green status. Sponsors remain accountable for their initiative's benefit realization for as long as it is in post-implementation monitoring, and a persistent Red status is expected to trigger a documented remediation plan.

Not every initiative reaches this stage in the same way. A rejected case never enters monitoring; a deferred case does not until it is re-presented and approved; and a pre-go-live case is tracked for delivery but not yet for benefits. Only initiatives that have gone live carry the benefits obligation, which is why the quarterly tracker at any given time reports hard data on a subset of the portfolio while carrying the remainder as informational. The PMO and FP&A jointly decide when a newly live initiative's first full quarter of tracking begins, normally the first complete quarter after go-live.

## Change Request and Addendum Process

Approved cases sometimes change. When a change to scope, cost, or benefit is material — Copperline's materiality threshold is a variance exceeding **10% of approved TCO** — the sponsor may not simply proceed. A formal addendum is required: a change request that restates the affected sections of the original case with revised figures, explains the drivers of the change, and is brought back to the Steering Committee. Where the revised ask crosses the $5M single-year threshold, the addendum also returns to the Board Finance & Audit Committee for re-ratification.

Once an addendum is approved, the original case is marked **superseded-by-addendum** and the addendum becomes the governing document of record. The ERP Replacement change request is the reference example of this process: it follows exactly the path described here, preserving the original case as history while re-ratifying the program on revised numbers. Changes below the materiality threshold do not require an addendum but must still be noted to the PMO and reflected in the next benefits-tracking cycle.

## Roles and Ownership

The PMO owns this policy and the template, screens submissions for conformance, and maintains the disposition record. FP&A owns the financial model and the benefits tracker. Sponsors own the substance of their cases and remain accountable through post-implementation monitoring. Internal Audit, under Grace Muthoni, periodically reviews the portfolio's adherence to this policy and may raise findings where the process is not being followed. Questions about interpretation of this guide should be directed to the Enterprise PMO.
