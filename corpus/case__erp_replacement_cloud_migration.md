# ERP Replacement Business Case: Legacy "Summit" to Ledgerline Cloud ERP

## Executive Summary

Copperline Retail Group, Inc. operates 214 specialty home and garden stores across 31 states, four distribution centers (Charlotte NC, Reno NV, Columbus OH, Allentown PA), and an e-commerce channel that now represents roughly 18% of the company's $1.92B FY2025 net revenue. The financial, inventory, and procurement backbone for that entire operation is "Summit," an on-premise ERP platform that Copperline first implemented seventeen years ago and has since extended with a lattice of custom code, bolt-on modules, and hand-built interfaces. Summit has been a workhorse, but it is now the single largest source of operational fragility and financial-reporting latency in the enterprise. This business case recommends its retirement and replacement with Ledgerline Cloud ERP, a modern SaaS platform selected through a competitive RFP process documented in the ERP Finalists Scorecard.

The proposed program carries a three-year total cost of ownership of $22.5M and is projected to deliver $29.7M in quantified benefit over the same window, producing a net present value of $4.9M at Copperline's standard 8% discount rate, an internal rate of return of 19%, and a payback period of 28 months. Because the single-year capital component exceeds the $5M threshold defined in the Business Case Review and Template Guide, this initiative requires ratification by the Board Finance & Audit Committee in addition to Tech Investment Steering Committee approval. The case is sponsored by Renata Alvarez, EVP & CFO, with the financial model prepared by Omar Delgado, Director of FP&A. It is submitted for the December 2024 approval cycle with a go-live target of Q4 2026.

The strategic argument is straightforward. Copperline's post-recapitalization growth thesis depends on tighter inventory turns, faster and more credible financial close, and an enterprise data foundation capable of supporting the omnichannel and customer-data investments already moving through the portfolio. Summit cannot deliver any of those things without escalating, and increasingly unrewarded, maintenance spend. Replacing it is not a discretionary modernization; it is a prerequisite for the operating model the company has told its owners and lenders it intends to run.

This case is deliberately conservative in its benefit assumptions and deliberately explicit about its risks. The largest of those risks — an implementation overrun concentrated in the integration workstream — is called out here at the outset because it is the variable most likely to move the outcome, and the governance model has been designed to manage it through staged capital release rather than a single up-front commitment. The Steering Committee and the Board Finance & Audit Committee are asked to approve a program, not merely a purchase: a multi-year, stage-gated replacement of the company's transactional core, with the authority to pause, re-scope, or re-baseline at each quarterly review preserved throughout.

## Problem & Strategic Context

Summit was commissioned when Copperline was a 60-store regional chain. It has been stretched, patched, and re-plumbed to serve a business more than three times that size, and the seams are showing in ways that now carry real financial and control consequences.

The first and most visible pain point is the close cycle. Summit posts general-ledger activity through overnight batch jobs; there is no real-time posting layer. The finance organization cannot see a reliable consolidated position until the batch completes, and any exception — a failed store upload, a mismatched intercompany entry, a currency or tax edge case — is not surfaced until the next morning, forcing a re-run and cascading the delay. The current monthly close runs nine business days. For a PE-owned business that owes monthly reporting packages to its sponsor and its lending syndicate, a nine-day close is both a governance liability and a drag on the FP&A team's capacity to do forward-looking work. During quarter-end, the close team routinely absorbs weekend and overnight hours simply to hit external deadlines.

The cost of that latency is not merely inconvenience. In the March 2024 close, a single Allentown DC upload failed silently during the batch window; the exception was not caught until the following afternoon, and by the time the re-run completed and the intercompany eliminations were re-validated, the controller's team had lost the better part of two days against an already tight external deadline. Episodes like that are not rare — they are a structural feature of a batch-only architecture that offers no live validation and no mid-cycle visibility. The corporate controller estimates that exception chasing and manual reconciliation consume roughly a third of the close team's effort each month, effort that produces no analysis and no insight, only a number that is finally trustworthy nine days after month-end. Every day of close latency is also a day that the FP&A organization spends assembling the past rather than modeling the future — a particularly poor trade for a business whose owners expect the finance function to be a source of forward-looking decision support.

The second pain point is inventory visibility. Summit maintains inventory balances per location but reconciles them centrally on the same nightly batch cadence. There is no real-time, cross-node view of on-hand and in-transit stock across the 214 stores and four distribution centers. Store associates cannot reliably promise availability from another location; the merchandising team plans replenishment against numbers that are, by construction, a day stale; and the e-commerce channel — now nearly a fifth of revenue — has to hold conservative safety-stock buffers because it cannot trust a live network position. The measured inventory record accuracy across the network currently sits at approximately 92%, which sounds high until it is multiplied across a $1.92B revenue base, where the resulting overstocks, markdowns, and phantom stockouts represent millions of dollars of avoidable margin leakage each year.

The third pain point is integration fragility. Over seventeen years, Summit has accumulated a dense web of custom-built integrations to surrounding systems — point of sale, warehouse management, the e-commerce platform, payroll, tax, banking, and vendor EDI among them. Many of these interfaces were written by staff who have long since left the company, are documented thinly if at all, and share no common middleware or error-handling standard. When one breaks, diagnosis is archaeological. The IT organization spends a disproportionate and growing share of its run budget simply keeping these interfaces alive, and every one of them is a latent single point of failure for a revenue-critical process.

The IT organization's own working inventory identifies six principal custom interfaces between Summit and the surrounding landscape, and the implementation scope, cost, and contingency in this case are sized against that count. The program team notes, with appropriate caution, that a legacy platform of Summit's age and customization history frequently harbors additional undocumented connective tissue that only a systematic, exhaustive integration-mapping exercise can fully surface. Sizing the integration workstream is therefore the single greatest source of estimation uncertainty in this case, which is precisely why implementation overrun — concentrated in that workstream — is named as the program's dominant risk and why a contingency reserve is carried against it.

The fourth pain point is vendor and platform risk. The Summit platform is approaching the end of its vendor-supported life. Security patching has slowed, the underlying database and middleware versions are trailing supported releases, and the pool of engineers — internal or contract — who genuinely understand the environment is shrinking every year. Continuing to run Summit means accepting a compounding operational risk that is not offset by any new capability.

Set against those liabilities is the strategic direction the company has already committed to. The Cloud Infrastructure Migration case, approved in November 2024, moves Copperline's core compute to a modern cloud foundation; leaving the ERP stranded on aging on-premise hardware undercuts that investment. The Omnichannel POS / Unified Commerce program and the Customer Data Platform initiative both assume a trustworthy, real-time enterprise data spine that Summit cannot provide. Replacing Summit with a cloud-native ERP is the keystone that makes the rest of the technology portfolio coherent rather than a set of modern applications bolted onto a brittle legacy core.

## Financial Modeling

The total cost of ownership is modeled across a three-year window (FY2025 through FY2027) and organized into six cost categories consistent with the Business Case Review policy. Figures are in millions of U.S. dollars.

| Cost Category | FY2025 | FY2026 | FY2027 | 3-Year Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Subscription & licensing | 2.4 | 2.8 | 2.9 | 8.1 |
| Implementation & integration | 3.2 | 3.8 | 0.6 | 7.6 |
| Data migration | 1.1 | 0.9 | 0.2 | 2.2 |
| Training & change management | 0.4 | 0.7 | 0.3 | 1.4 |
| Infrastructure decommissioning | 0.1 | 0.3 | 0.5 | 0.9 |
| Contingency | 0.8 | 0.9 | 0.6 | 2.3 |
| **Total cost of ownership** | **8.0** | **9.4** | **5.1** | **22.5** |

Subscription and licensing reflects Ledgerline's per-module SaaS pricing scaled to Copperline's user counts and transaction volumes, with the modest year-over-year increase driven by contracted uplift and the phased activation of modules as the rollout progresses. Implementation and integration — the largest single category — covers the systems-integration partner, the rebuild of interfaces to the surrounding application landscape, configuration, and testing; it is deliberately front-loaded into FY2025 and FY2026 and tapers to a stabilization tail in FY2027. Data migration funds the extraction, cleansing, mapping, and validation of master and transactional data out of Summit. Training and change management covers role-based enablement for finance, merchandising, supply-chain, and store users. Infrastructure decommissioning captures the wind-down of Summit's on-premise footprint and the retirement of its supporting middleware and database licenses, ramping as the legacy environment is progressively switched off. Contingency is held at approximately 10% of program cost, consistent with policy guidance for a program of this complexity and integration surface.

The benefit stream, detailed in the following section, ramps as functionality goes live and users adopt it, and is weighted toward the back half of the window as the real-time close and inventory capabilities reach steady state. Discounting the net cash flows at the company's standard 8% rate yields the following program-level metrics.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| 3-year total cost of ownership | $22.5M |
| Projected 3-year benefit | $29.7M |
| Net present value (8% discount rate) | $4.9M |
| Internal rate of return | 19% |
| Payback period | 28 months |

The 28-month payback reflects the front-loaded cost profile: the program consumes cash through FY2025 and into FY2026 before the benefit stream overtakes cumulative outlay in the second half of the horizon. The positive NPV and a 19% IRR comfortably clearing the 8% hurdle rate confirm that the investment creates value even under conservative benefit assumptions, as the scenario modeling below demonstrates.

A note on methodology is warranted given the board-level scrutiny this ask will receive. The model treats the SaaS subscription as an operating cost recognized in the year incurred rather than a capitalized asset, consistent with the platform's cloud delivery model and with the treatment applied to the Cloud Infrastructure Migration case. Implementation and integration costs are modeled on the resource-loaded statements of work negotiated with the systems-integration partner, not on top-down benchmarks, which is why the category is the most precisely estimated of the six despite being the largest. The benefit stream is discounted on the same annual basis and the same 8% rate; no terminal value or benefit continuation beyond the three-year window is credited, which makes the reported NPV a conservative floor rather than a full-lifecycle valuation. Were the model extended to reflect the platform's expected useful life, the NPV would be materially higher; the three-year framing is used deliberately to align with the portfolio's standard evaluation window and to avoid over-crediting distant, less certain benefits.

## Benefits Analysis

The benefit case rests on five quantified value streams, each tied to a measurable baseline, an accountable owner, and an expected realization window. Benefit figures below represent the cumulative three-year contribution of each stream and sum to the $29.7M program total.

| Benefit Stream | Baseline | Projected Improvement | Evidence Basis | Owner | Timing | 3-Yr Value |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Finance close & labor efficiency | 9-business-day monthly close; heavy overtime | Close reduced to 4 business days; redeployment of ~30% of close-cycle effort | Ledgerline real-time posting benchmarks; internal time-and-motion study of FY2024 close | Renata Alvarez (CFO) | Ramps from go-live; steady state by mid-FY2027 | $6.8M |
| Inventory carrying-cost reduction | 92% inventory record accuracy; conservative safety stock | Accuracy to 98%; reduced buffer stock via trusted network position | Merchandising cycle-count data; peer benchmarks for real-time ERP inventory | Devon Marsh (VP Supply Chain) | Begins post-DC rollout; full effect FY2027 | $9.4M |
| Markdown & stockout reduction | Day-stale replenishment signals; recurring localized stockouts | Real-time cross-node visibility reduces both markdowns and lost sales | Historical markdown analysis; e-commerce fill-rate logs | Sasha Kim (VP Digital Commerce) | Phased with store/e-comm cutover | $7.2M |
| IT infrastructure & support savings | High run-cost for Summit interfaces, hardware, and legacy licenses | Decommissioning of on-premise footprint; consolidated cloud-native interface layer | IT run-cost ledger; Summit maintenance and license contracts | Marcus Whitfield (CIO) | Ramps as Summit is switched off | $4.1M |
| Audit & compliance cost avoidance | Recurring control findings tied to manual interfaces and batch reconciliations | Automated controls and audit trails reduce finding volume and remediation effort | Internal Audit finding history; SOX control inventory | Grace Muthoni (Chief Audit Executive) | From first full post-go-live audit cycle | $2.2M |

The two largest streams — inventory carrying-cost reduction and markdown/stockout reduction — together account for more than half of the benefit and derive from the same underlying capability: a trustworthy, real-time, network-wide inventory position. This is the single most important thing Summit cannot do and Ledgerline can, and it is why the supply-chain and digital-commerce organizations are as invested in this program as the finance organization that sponsors it. The finance close and labor-efficiency benefit is the most directly attributable and the fastest to materialize, since it follows almost immediately from replacing overnight batch posting with real-time general-ledger activity. The IT and audit streams are smaller but among the most defensible, as they are grounded in contracts and finding histories rather than behavioral projections.

A word on the credibility of the two inventory-driven streams, since together they carry the case. The improvement from 92% to 98% inventory record accuracy is not an aspirational round number; it is the accuracy band that peer retailers operating real-time, perpetual-inventory ERP platforms consistently sustain, and the merchandising organization's own cycle-count data confirms that the residual 8% error today is dominated by timing artifacts of the nightly batch rather than genuine physical loss. Removing the batch removes most of that error by construction. The carrying-cost benefit then follows from two mechanisms: the ability to hold less safety stock once the network position can be trusted, and the reduction in emergency inter-store and inter-DC transfers that today paper over a position no one quite believes. The markdown-and-stockout stream is the more behavioral of the two and is deliberately sized below the theoretical ceiling implied by the accuracy gain, precisely because it depends on merchandising and store teams changing how they plan and promise availability. That conservatism is reflected in the pessimistic scenario, where only a partial inventory benefit is credited and the program still clears its hurdle rate.

Each benefit stream carries a named, accountable owner rather than being pooled under the finance sponsor, a structure the Enterprise PMO insists upon so that realization can be tracked against a specific executive at each quarterly Steering Committee review. Baselines for every stream will be instrumented and locked before cutover, so that post-go-live measurement is against a defensible starting point rather than a reconstructed one.

## Risk Assessment

The program's risks are assessed across the five standard categories defined in the Business Case Review policy. Each is rated for likelihood and impact and paired with the mitigations built into the program plan and budget.

| Risk Category | Likelihood | Impact | Description & Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Implementation overrun | Medium | High | A program of this integration surface can exceed schedule and budget, particularly on interface rebuilds. Mitigation: phased rollout, a 10% contingency reserve, fixed-scope statements of work with the SI partner, and stage-gated Steering Committee checkpoints. |
| Benefit-realization shortfall | Medium | High | Projected benefits — especially inventory and markdown streams — depend on process adoption and disciplined master-data hygiene. Mitigation: named benefit owners, a benefits-realization tracker reviewed at each committee, and baseline instrumentation captured before cutover. |
| Data-quality degradation | Medium | Medium | Seventeen years of accreted Summit data includes duplicates, orphaned records, and inconsistent master data that could corrupt the new platform. Mitigation: a dedicated data-migration workstream with cleansing, mapping, and multi-pass validation gates before each cutover. |
| Adoption failure | Medium | High | Store, finance, and supply-chain users accustomed to Summit workflows may resist or work around the new system. Mitigation: role-based training funded in the model, a change-management workstream, super-user networks per region, and post-go-live hypercare. |
| Vendor viability | Low | Medium | Dependence on Ledgerline Systems as a SaaS provider introduces concentration risk. Mitigation: Ledgerline was assessed as financially sound in the finalist scorecard; contractual source-code escrow, data-portability, and service-level terms are included in the master agreement. |

The two risks carrying the highest combined exposure are implementation overrun and benefit-realization shortfall. Both are actively managed through the governance model rather than assumed away: the phased plan creates natural stage gates at which the Steering Committee can pause, re-scope, or re-baseline before further capital is committed. Notably, the implementation-overrun risk is concentrated in the integration workstream, where the count and complexity of legacy Summit interfaces is the dominant variable — a point that later program discovery would prove material.

## Scenario Modeling

To test the durability of the recommendation, the financial model was run under three scenarios spanning a reasonable range of implementation cost and benefit-realization outcomes. The base case carries the pinned program figures; the optimistic and pessimistic cases flex implementation cost, adoption speed, and benefit ramp.

| Scenario | Assumptions | 3-Yr TCO | 3-Yr Benefit | NPV (8%) | IRR | Payback |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Base | On-plan delivery; benefits ramp as modeled | $22.5M | $29.7M | $4.9M | 19% | 28 mo |
| Optimistic | Clean data migration; faster adoption; full inventory benefit by mid-FY2027 | $21.0M | $33.1M | $7.6M | 26% | 23 mo |
| Pessimistic | Integration overrun; slower adoption; partial inventory benefit | $25.4M | $25.9M | $1.3M | 11% | 34 mo |

The recommendation holds across the full range. Even in the pessimistic scenario — which assumes a meaningful integration overrun and a muted benefit ramp — the program preserves a positive NPV and an IRR above the 8% hurdle, meaning it still creates value for Copperline's owners. The optimistic case, by contrast, illustrates the upside available if data migration is clean and the supply-chain organization realizes the inventory benefit ahead of plan. The spread between the scenarios is driven primarily by the integration workstream and the pace of inventory-benefit realization, which is why both are called out explicitly in the risk assessment and instrumented in the benefits tracker.

It is worth naming what the pessimistic scenario does and does not contemplate. Its $25.4M cost assumes an integration overrun of roughly 13% against the base plan — a plausible outcome if the interface count or complexity proves greater than the working inventory suggests, and the kind of contingency the reserve is intended to cover. It does not contemplate a wholesale doubling of the integration surface; a discovery of that magnitude would exceed the modeled range and would properly trigger a formal change request and a return to the Steering Committee — and, given the board threshold, to the Board Finance & Audit Committee — rather than being absorbed silently within the approved envelope. The governance model is explicitly built to handle that possibility: the phased, stage-gated structure ensures that any such re-baselining surfaces at a quarterly review, with capital release paused until the revised case is approved. In other words, the scenario range bounds the outcomes the program can absorb on its own authority, and the governance path bounds everything beyond it.

## Recommendation & Governance

FP&A and the Enterprise PMO recommend that the Tech Investment Steering Committee approve the replacement of the legacy Summit ERP with Ledgerline Cloud ERP at a three-year total cost of ownership of $22.5M, targeting go-live in Q4 2026. The investment delivers a projected $29.7M in three-year benefit, a net present value of $4.9M at the 8% discount rate, a 19% internal rate of return, and a 28-month payback, and it is the keystone enabler for the company's cloud, omnichannel, and customer-data initiatives.

Because the program's single-year capital component exceeds the $5M threshold established in the Business Case Review and Template Guide, Steering Committee approval alone is not sufficient. The initiative requires ratification by the Board Finance & Audit Committee. Harold Whitcombe, Independent Director and Chair of the Board Finance & Audit Committee, is the ratifying authority for this capital ask. Upon Steering Committee approval in the December 2024 cycle, the case will be advanced to the Finance & Audit Committee for board-level ratification, after which the program will move into execution under standard portfolio governance, with stage-gate reviews at each quarterly Steering Committee meeting and benefit tracking through the portfolio benefits-realization process.

The sponsor accepts accountability for benefit delivery and for bringing any material change in scope, cost, or timeline back to the Steering Committee, and — where the revised ask remains above the board threshold — to the Board Finance & Audit Committee for supplemental ratification.
